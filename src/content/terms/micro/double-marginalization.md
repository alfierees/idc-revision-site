Double Marginalization occurs when two or more firms in a vertical supply chain — or two firms each controlling a [[Perfect Complements|complementary]] component — each apply a markup to the final consumer price *independently*. Each firm sets its price to maximise its own profit, ignoring the negative [[Pricing Externality]] it imposes on the other firms because its higher price reduces the total quantity all firms share. The result with $N$ symmetric complementary firms is
$$P^N = \frac{AN}{N+1}, \quad Q^N = \frac{A}{N+1},$$
so total price climbs toward the choke price as $N$ grows, and industry profit collapses.

## When to use

Invoke double marginalization to explain why decentralised firms over-price relative to an integrated monopolist, why more independent complementary firms make things *worse* for everyone (consumers and firms), and why [[Vertical Integration]] or two-part-tariff contracts are efficiency-enhancing remedies. It also explains the producer→retailer wedge in Topic 2's vertical-relations section (Q^VR = (A−k)/4 vs Q^M = (A−k)/2).
