A firm's **cost function** $TC(Q)$ specifies the total cost of producing each output level $Q$. It decomposes into **explicit** costs (cash outflows: wages, rent, materials) and **implicit** costs ([[Opportunity Cost|opportunity costs]] with no money changing hands), and into **fixed** costs (independent of $Q$) and **variable** costs (rising with $Q$). The two textbook marginal-cost shapes are **Type 1 (constant MC)** — $TC = F + cQ$, so $MC = c$ is a horizontal line — and **Type 2 (increasing MC)** — convex $TC$, so $MC$ rises with output (diminishing marginal product of inputs).

## When to use

Cost functions are the supply-side primitive behind every Topic 2 result: the monopolist solves $MR(Q) = MC(Q)$, so the shape of $MC$ determines whether the optimum is interior or at a capacity bound. The constant-MC case is the workhorse for closed-form linear-demand monopoly problems (see [[Solving a linear-demand monopoly]]). Increasing-MC cases show up in [[Cournot Competition]] with asymmetric costs — the firm with the lower MC schedule produces more in equilibrium. Always distinguish *avoidable* from [[Sunk Cost|sunk]] costs when computing forward-looking optima.
