  - id: q1
    title: "Q1 — Full accounting cycle: journal entries, statements & ratios (40 pts)"
    text: |
      **Meadowlark Inc.** is incorporated on **1 January 2025**. During its first year the following events occur (all amounts in $):

      1. **1 Jan** — Founders contribute **$200,000** cash for **200,000** common shares (**$0.01 par**).
      2. **1 Jan** — Buy equipment for **$12,000** cash; **4-year** useful life, straight-line, no salvage.
      3. **1 Feb** — Pay **$18,000** for a **12-month** insurance policy (coverage Feb 2025 – Jan 2026).
      4. **1 Mar** — Draw a **$100,000** bank loan at **6% p.a.**; interest is paid each **1 January** for the prior year, principal is a **bullet due 2028**.
      5. Office rent is **$4,000/month**, paid on the 1st of each month.
      6. Payroll is **$10,000/month**, accrued at each month-end and paid on the **5th of the following month**.
      7. Services delivered in the year total **$320,000** (invoiced **Net 30**); **$280,000** is collected in cash by year-end, leaving the December invoice in receivables.
      8. Corporate tax rate is **23%**, paid the following year.

      Required: **(a)** the headline journal entries, **(b)** the Income Statement for 2025, **(c)** the Balance Sheet at 31 Dec 2025, and **(d)** the current ratio, quick ratio, debt-to-equity, net profit margin and ROE.
    solution: |
      > Tests the whole cycle from [[Double Entry Bookkeeping|double-entry]] through the [[Income Statement]], [[Balance Sheet]] and [[Ratio Analysis|ratios]] — the 40-point backbone of the paper. Theory: [[Session 2 - Accounting Fundamentals]] and [[Session 5 - Financial Statements Analysis]].

      ### (a) Headline journal entries

      The credited account is indented; in every entry **total debits = total credits**.

      | Date | Account | Dr ($) | Cr ($) |
      |------|---------|------:|------:|
      | 1 Jan | Cash | 200,000 | |
      | | &emsp;Common Stock (200,000 × $0.01) | | 2,000 |
      | | &emsp;APIC — Common | | 198,000 |
      | | *Founders' contribution — par vs premium split* | | |
      | 1 Jan | Equipment | 12,000 | |
      | | &emsp;Cash | | 12,000 |
      | | *Capitalise: useful life > 1 yr* | | |
      | 1 Feb | Prepaid Insurance | 18,000 | |
      | | &emsp;Cash | | 18,000 |
      | | *Future benefit → asset, expensed as consumed* | | |
      | 1 Mar | Cash | 100,000 | |
      | | &emsp;Loan Payable | | 100,000 |
      | | *Borrowing is a liability, not revenue* | | |
      | monthly | Rent Expense | 4,000 | |
      | | &emsp;Cash | | 4,000 |
      | | *Paid on the 1st → no accrual* | | |
      | month-end | Payroll Expense | 10,000 | |
      | | &emsp;Accrued Payroll Payable | | 10,000 |
      | | *Earned this month; paid the 5th of next month* | | |
      | on delivery | Accounts Receivable | 320,000 | |
      | | &emsp;Service Revenue | | 320,000 |
      | | *Recognised when delivered (Net 30)* | | |

      **Year-end adjusting entries:**

      | Adjustment | Debit | Credit | Calculation |
      |---|---|---|---|
      | Depreciation | Depreciation Expense $3,000 | Accumulated Depreciation $3,000 | $12,000 ÷ 4 |
      | Insurance used | Insurance Expense $16,500 | Prepaid Insurance $16,500 | $18,000 × 11/12 (Feb–Dec) |
      | Accrue interest | Interest Expense $5,000 | Interest Payable $5,000 | $100,000 × 6% × 10/12 (Mar–Dec) |
      | Accrue tax | Tax Expense $29,325 | Tax Payable $29,325 | $127,500 × 23% |

      > [!warning] The three cash-timing traps
      > **Dec payroll** ($10,000) is expensed but sits as a payable — it's paid 5 Jan. **Interest and tax** are 2025 expenses but paid in 2026, so they too are year-end liabilities. **December revenue** ($40,000) is earned and in AR but never touches 2025 cash.

      ### (b) Income Statement — year ended 31 Dec 2025

      > [!example] MEADOWLARK INC. — Income Statement 2025
      > | | Amount | Basis |
      > |---|---:|---|
      > | Service Revenue | 320,000 | recognised on delivery |
      > | Payroll Expense | (120,000) | $10,000 × 12 |
      > | Rent Expense | (48,000) | $4,000 × 12 |
      > | Insurance Expense | (16,500) | 11 months consumed |
      > | Depreciation Expense | (3,000) | full year |
      > | **Operating Income** | **132,500** | |
      > | Interest Expense | (5,000) | 10 months accrued |
      > | **Pre-tax Income** | **127,500** | |
      > | Tax @ 23% | (29,325) | accrued, paid 2026 |
      > | **NET INCOME** | **98,175** | → Retained Earnings |

      ### (c) Balance Sheet at 31 Dec 2025

      **Cash** = 200,000 + 100,000 + 280,000 (in) − 12,000 − 18,000 − 48,000 − 110,000 (out) = **$392,000**. (Interest and tax are paid in 2026, so no cash leaves in 2025; 11 payroll runs are paid, December's is not.)

      > [!example] MEADOWLARK INC. — Balance Sheet, 31 Dec 2025
      > | | Amount |
      > |---|---:|
      > | **Current Assets** | |
      > | &emsp;Cash | 392,000 |
      > | &emsp;Accounts Receivable | 40,000 |
      > | &emsp;Prepaid Insurance | 1,500 |
      > | **Non-Current Assets** | |
      > | &emsp;Equipment, at cost | 12,000 |
      > | &emsp;Less: accumulated depreciation | (3,000) |
      > | &emsp;Equipment, net book value | 9,000 |
      > | **TOTAL ASSETS** | **442,500** |
      > | **Current Liabilities** | |
      > | &emsp;Accrued Payroll Payable | 10,000 |
      > | &emsp;Interest Payable | 5,000 |
      > | &emsp;Tax Payable | 29,325 |
      > | **Non-Current Liabilities** | |
      > | &emsp;Loan Payable (due 2028) | 100,000 |
      > | **TOTAL LIABILITIES** | **144,325** |
      > | **Shareholders' Equity** | |
      > | &emsp;Common Stock (par) | 2,000 |
      > | &emsp;APIC — Common | 198,000 |
      > | &emsp;Retained Earnings | 98,175 |
      > | **TOTAL EQUITY** | **298,175** |
      > | **TOTAL LIAB. + EQUITY** | **442,500** ✓ |

      ### (d) Ratios

      | Ratio | Working | Value |
      |---|---|---:|
      | [[Current Ratio\|Current ratio]] | 433,500 ÷ 44,325 | **9.78** |
      | [[Quick Ratio\|Quick ratio]] | (392,000 + 40,000) ÷ 44,325 | **9.75** |
      | Debt-to-equity | 100,000 ÷ 298,175 | **0.34** |
      | Net profit margin | 98,175 ÷ 320,000 | **30.7%** |
      | [[ROE]] | 98,175 ÷ 298,175 | **32.9%** |

      *Current liabilities = 10,000 + 5,000 + 29,325 = $44,325; the loan is non-current. Current assets also include the $1,500 prepaid, which the quick ratio excludes.*

      > [!tip] What the ratios say
      > Meadowlark is **extremely liquid** (current ratio ~9.8×) because it is freshly funded and holds most of its raise in cash. Leverage is modest (0.34×) and it is already **profitable** with a 30.7% net margin — a healthy first-year picture, though the liquidity ratios will fall fast as it starts spending the raise.

  - id: q2
    title: "Q2 — SAFE conversion & cap table (15 pts)"
    text: |
      **NimbusAI** is founded with **500,000** common shares held by the founders.

      - A **SAFE** is signed with **Investor A** for **$1,000,000**, with an **$8,000,000 valuation cap** *or* a **20% discount** (whichever is better for the investor).
      - Later, **Investor B** leads a priced round investing **$4,000,000** at a **$16,000,000 pre-money** valuation, which triggers the SAFE conversion.

      Required: the SAFE conversion price and shares, Investor B's shares, and the resulting **cap table** with ownership percentages.
    solution: |
      > A [[SAFE]] converts on the priced round: compare the cap and the discount and give the investor the **lower** price. Theory: [[Session 3 - Raising Debt and Capital]]; this is the same mechanism as [[Session 9 - Recap]] Ex. 5.

      **Step 1 — Investor A's SAFE conversion.** Take the lower of the two prices:

      | Method | Price per share | |
      |---|---|---|
      | 20% discount on the round | ($16,000,000 ÷ 500,000) × 80% = $32.00 × 0.8 = **$25.60** | worse for A |
      | **$8M cap** | $8,000,000 ÷ 500,000 = **$16.00** | **cap wins** ✓ |

      → Investor A receives $1,000,000 ÷ $16.00 = **62,500 preferred shares**.

      **Step 2 — Investor B's price.** The round is priced on the shares outstanding **before the SAFE converts** (the founders' 500,000):

      $$\text{Price}_B = \frac{\$16{,}000{,}000}{500{,}000} = \$32.00/\text{share}$$

      → Investor B receives $4,000,000 ÷ $32 = **125,000 preferred shares**.

      > [!example] NimbusAI — cap table after the round
      > | Shareholder | Shares | Investment | Ownership |
      > |---|---:|---:|---:|
      > | Founders (common) | 500,000 | $100,000\* | **72.7%** |
      > | Investor A (SAFE → preferred) | 62,500 | $1,000,000 | **9.1%** |
      > | Investor B (preferred) | 125,000 | $4,000,000 | **18.2%** |
      > | **Total** | **687,500** | | **100%** |
      >
      > \*Nominal founder cost; not part of the round math.

      > [!tip] Why the price uses the pre-SAFE share count
      > The round is priced on the shares outstanding **before** the SAFE converts (500,000), so Investor B pays **$32/share**. The SAFE's 62,500 shares then convert alongside and dilute everyone, so Investor B's final stake is **18.2%** — below the naive $4M ÷ $20M = 20%, because the converting SAFE dilutes the new investor too.

      > [!warning] Exam trap — always compute both terms
      > Students reflexively apply the discount. Here the **cap ($16.00) beats the discount ($25.60)**, so the cap governs. Compute both and take whichever gives the investor **more shares** (the lower price).
    related_terms:
      - safe
      - cap-table
      - pre-money-valuation
      - post-money-valuation
      - preferred-stock
  - id: q3
    title: "Q3 — Business combination & goodwill (15 pts)"
    text: |
      On **1 July 2025**, **Orchard Corp** acquires **80%** of **Pearl Ltd** for **$6,000,000** cash, obtaining control. At the acquisition date, Pearl's **identifiable assets** have a fair value of **$7,000,000** and its **liabilities** a fair value of **$2,000,000**. The fair value of the **20% non-controlling interest** is **$1,400,000**.

      Required: compute goodwill under both the **full (fair-value) goodwill** method and the **partial (proportionate)** method, give the acquisition journal entry, and explain how goodwill is treated after acquisition.
    solution: |
      > Obtaining control triggers the [[Acquisition Method]] (IFRS 3). Theory: [[Session 7 - Business Combination]]; compare [[Session 9 - Recap]] Ex. 8.

      **Net identifiable assets at fair value** = $7,000,000 − $2,000,000 = **$5,000,000**.

      **Full (fair-value) goodwill** — value the whole entity, including NCI, at fair value:

      | ($) | Amount |
      |---|---:|
      | Consideration transferred (80%) | 6,000,000 |
      | + Fair value of NCI (20%) | 1,400,000 |
      | **A — Total** | **7,400,000** |
      | − Net identifiable assets at FV | (5,000,000) |
      | **Goodwill (full)** | **2,400,000** |

      **Partial (proportionate) goodwill** — measure NCI at its share of net identifiable assets instead:

      $$\text{Goodwill}_{\text{partial}} = \$6{,}000{,}000 - (80\% \times \$5{,}000{,}000) = 6{,}000{,}000 - 4{,}000{,}000 = \$2{,}000{,}000$$

      The **$400,000 difference** is exactly the goodwill attributable to the NCI, which the full method recognises and the partial method does not.

      > [!example] Acquisition journal entry (full-goodwill basis, $)
      > | Account | Dr | Cr |
      > |---|---:|---:|
      > | Identifiable assets — at FV | 7,000,000 | |
      > | Goodwill | 2,400,000 | |
      > | &emsp;Liabilities — at FV | | 2,000,000 |
      > | &emsp;Cash | | 6,000,000 |
      > | &emsp;Non-Controlling Interest (equity) | | 1,400,000 |
      >
      > *Check: debits 9,400,000 = credits 9,400,000.* ✓

      > [!tip] After the acquisition
      > [[Goodwill]] is **not amortised** — it is carried at cost and **tested for impairment** at least annually. [[Non-Controlling Interest|NCI]] sits **within equity** on the consolidated balance sheet, and Pearl's post-acquisition profit is split 80% to Orchard / 20% to NCI.

  - id: q4
    title: "Q4 — True/False with explanation (30 pts)"
    text: |
      For each of the following 10 statements, indicate whether it is **True** or **False** and give a one-line justification. (3 points each.)

      1. Under accrual accounting, revenue is recognised when cash is received.
      2. Depreciation is a non-cash expense: it reduces net income but does not directly reduce cash in the period it is recorded.
      3. A SAFE is recorded as equity on the balance sheet at the moment the cash is received.
      4. Under the equity method, a dividend received from an associate is recorded as dividend income in the P&L.
      5. Goodwill arising in a business combination is amortised over its useful life.
      6. When a SAFE has both a valuation cap and a discount, the investor converts at whichever gives them the lower price per share.
      7. No journal entry is recorded on the grant date of employee stock options; compensation expense is recognised over the vesting period.
      8. A company can report positive net income for a year while generating negative operating cash flow.
      9. The quick ratio is always greater than or equal to the current ratio.
      10. In a DCF valuation, a higher discount rate (WACC) increases the present value of the company.
    solution: |
      > Each statement isolates one core idea from across the course. The trap answers are the "intuitive but wrong" ones (3, 4, 5, 9, 10).

      1. **FALSE.** Under [[Accrual Accounting]] revenue is recognised when it is **earned** (control of the good/service transfers), not when cash arrives. Cash timing is a separate question.

      2. **TRUE.** [[Depreciation]] spreads an asset's cost over its life; the cash already left when the asset was bought. It lowers profit but is added back in the cash-flow statement.

      3. **FALSE.** A [[SAFE]] is a **liability** when the cash comes in — the company owes future shares. It becomes equity only **on conversion** at the priced round.

      4. **FALSE.** Under the [[Equity Method]] a dividend is a **return of capital** that **reduces the carrying value of the investment**, not P&L income. (Income comes from the investor's share of the associate's profit.)

      5. **FALSE.** [[Goodwill]] is **not amortised**. It is carried at cost and **tested for impairment** at least annually.

      6. **TRUE.** The investor gets the **more favourable (lower)** conversion price — whichever of the cap or the discount yields **more shares**. Always compute both.

      7. **TRUE.** The grant date is only the **measurement date** ([[Black-Scholes Model|Black-Scholes]] fixes the fair value). Expense is recognised over the [[Vesting|vesting]] period as the employee earns the award; the credit goes to APIC, not a liability.

      8. **TRUE.** Profit ≠ cash. A build-up in receivables (revenue earned but uncollected) or prepayments can make [[Cash Flows Statement|operating cash flow]] negative even in a profitable year.

      9. **FALSE.** The [[Quick Ratio|quick ratio]] excludes inventory and prepaid expenses from current assets, so it is **less than or equal to** the [[Current Ratio|current ratio]], never greater.

      10. **FALSE.** A higher [[WACC|discount rate]] discounts future cash flows **more heavily**, so it **lowers** present value. Higher risk → lower valuation.

      > [!tip] How this question is marked
      > On the real paper, the **verdict alone earns little** — the marks are in the one-line reason. "False — a SAFE is a liability until it converts" scores; a bare "False" does not.
