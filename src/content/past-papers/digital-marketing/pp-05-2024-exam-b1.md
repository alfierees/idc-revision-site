    title: "Q1 — Linear viewing"
    text: |
      Which of the following best defines the term Linear Viewing?
    options:
      - label: "a"
        text: |
          View content on the platform for which it was first produced.
        correct: false
        why: |
          This describes *where* you watch (the original platform), not *when*. Linear viewing is defined by timing — watching at the publisher's scheduled broadcast — not by the platform.
      - label: "b"
        text: |
          Watching live broadcasts such as news and sports on TV.
        correct: false
        why: |
          Tempting near-synonym: live TV usually *is* linear, but the defining feature is watching at the broadcaster's scheduled time, not the genre. Naming news/sports describes examples, not the concept.
      - label: "c"
        text: |
          Watch content on social networks dedicated to video such as TikTok or YouTube.
        correct: false
        why: |
          This is **on-demand / non-linear** viewing — you choose when to watch — the opposite of linear.
      - label: "d"
        text: |
          Viewing content at the time of its original distribution or broadcast, as defined by the content publisher.
        correct: true
        why: |
          Linear viewing means watching content at the time of its original broadcast, on the schedule set by the publisher — not on-demand.
    solution: |
      The trap is option **b**. Both b and d involve real-time viewing, so it's easy to pick "live news and sports." But *linear* is defined by **whose schedule you're on** — the publisher's original broadcast time — not by the content type. Option d states that timing rule directly; b only gives an example of it. The contrast term is **non-linear / on-demand** (option c), where the *viewer* chooses the moment.

  - id: q10
    title: "Q10 — Analyzing a sponsored campaign"
    text: |
      Which of the following could be used to analyze the performance of a sponsored campaign in social media?
    options:
      - label: "a"
        text: |
          MAU.
        correct: false
        why: |
          **MAU** = Monthly Active Users — a *platform / product* usage metric (how many unique people use the app in a month). It measures the platform's health, not the performance of one ad campaign.
      - label: "b"
        text: |
          CTR.
        correct: true
        why: |
          **CTR** = Click-Through Rate = clicks ÷ impressions. It directly measures how well a sponsored ad drives users to act, so it's the natural KPI for a social campaign.
      - label: "c"
        text: |
          OMTM.
        correct: false
        why: |
          **OMTM** = One Metric That Matters — the single guiding "north-star" metric for a company's *current stage* (e.g. activation, retention). It's a strategic focus device, not a specific campaign-performance measure.
      - label: "d"
        text: |
          DAU.
        correct: false
        why: |
          **DAU** = Daily Active Users — like MAU, a *platform* usage metric (daily unique users), not a measure of an individual ad campaign.
    solution: |
      The trap is that **all four are real metrics** — so you have to match the *level*, not just recognise the acronym. They sit at three different levels:

      - **CTR** — a *campaign-level* ad metric (per impression/click). ✅
      - **MAU / DAU** — *platform / product* usage metrics (how many people use the app).
      - **OMTM** — a *strategic* focus metric for the whole company at a given stage.

      Only **CTR** operates at the level of an individual **sponsored campaign**, which is why it's the answer. (Retention, by the way, is roughly **DAU ÷ MAU** — see the metrics section in [[Digital Marketing Lecture 2]].)

      Amit and Mika reviewed the Google Analytics data of their company's website, to which the PPC campaign of Display ads refers.
      Amit: In my opinion, our campaign is very successful, given the Page Visited figures, which are higher than prior to the campaign.
      Mika: It's true that our Page Visited figures are higher, yet I still think your conclusion is wrong.
      Which of the following could be an argument that Mika would use to explain her claim to Amir?
    options:
      - label: "a"
        text: |
          The Opt. out rate in the relevant period was about 12%.
        correct: false
        why: |
          A modest opt-out rate isn't evidence the campaign *failed* — if anything it's unremarkable. It doesn't support Mika's claim that Amit is wrong.
      - label: "b"
        text: |
          The CTR of the campaign was about 25% higher than that of the previous one.
        correct: false
        why: |
          A *higher* CTR signals the campaign performed **better** — this supports Amit's success conclusion, the opposite of Mika's argument.
      - label: "c"
        text: |
          The time on site during the relevant period was about 7 minutes.
        correct: false
        why: |
          A long time-on-site indicates *engaged* visitors — again evidence *for* success, not for Mika's doubt.
      - label: "d"
        text: |
          None of the other answers is correct.
        correct: true
        why: |
          A low opt-out rate, a higher CTR, and a long time-on-site would all support the campaign being successful, so none of them backs Mika's claim that the conclusion is wrong.
    solution: |
      Work backwards from what Mika needs. She's arguing the campaign was **not** successful despite higher page visits. So the right answer must be a fact that *undercuts* success. Test each: a low opt-out rate (a), a higher CTR (b), and a long time-on-site (c) all point *toward* the campaign working. Since every concrete option supports Amit, **none of them** can be Mika's argument → **d**. The lesson: more page visits alone doesn't prove success — but the distractors here don't disprove it either.

  - id: q17
    title: "Q17 — Yuk yoghurt media-buying model"
    text: |
      An organic yoghurt company approached influencers on Instagram to enthusiastically tell their followers about their new product, Yuk, a series of fruit flavored yogurts with chia seeds, and invite them to use a dedicated "coupon code" to receive a 15% discount on the company's website. The payment to the influencers was based on the revenue generated by their coupon code.
      Which of the following answers describes the media buying model the company chose to use?
    options:
      - label: "a"
        text: |
          CPL model.
        correct: false
        why: |
          **CPL** (Cost per Lead) pays per captured lead — a sign-up or enquiry. Here payment is tied to actual **revenue/sales**, which is further down the funnel than a lead.
      - label: "b"
        text: |
          CPC model.
        correct: false
        why: |
          **CPC** (Cost per Click) pays per click regardless of what happens next. The influencers were paid on revenue generated, not on clicks.
      - label: "c"
        text: |
          CPA model.
        correct: true
        why: |
          Paying influencers based on revenue generated by their coupon code ties payment to a completed action/sale — the defining feature of the CPA (cost-per-action) model.
      - label: "d"
        text: |
          FAST model.
        correct: false
        why: |
          **FAST** (Free Ad-Supported Streaming TV) is a *channel/content* type, not a media-buying/payment model — it doesn't describe how the influencers were paid.
    solution: |
      Follow the payment trigger. The influencers were paid **based on the revenue generated by their coupon code** — i.e. only when a follower completed a purchase. That ties payment to a completed **action/sale**, which is the defining feature of the **CPA (Cost per Action)** model. Contrast the funnel: **CPC** pays on the click, **CPL** pays on the lead, **CPA** pays on the conversion. FAST isn't a payment model at all.
