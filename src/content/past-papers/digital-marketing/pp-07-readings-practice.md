    title: "Q1 — Doers vs intenders (Kariv et al.)"
    text: |
      According to Kariv, Krueger, Cisneros and Kashy-Rosenbaum in "Embedding the marketing angle into the pursuit of entrepreneurial propensity" (2025), what did their discriminant analysis reveal about the three entrepreneurial-propensity groups?
    options:
      - label: "a"
        text: |
          Perceived feasibility and desirability separated intenders from non-intenders, while stakeholder support separated those who acted from everyone else.
        correct: false
        why: |
          This reverses the finding: the psychological constructs separated action from non-action, and the market-support constructs separated the two non-action groups.
      - label: "b"
        text: |
          Buffering support from institutions carried the highest discriminant loading, ahead of market feasibility and desirability.
        correct: false
        why: |
          Buffering-I was real but the weakest significant discriminator; Market Feasibility had the highest loading, followed by Market Desirability and Bridging-P.
      - label: "c"
        text: |
          All predictors differentiated the three groups to a similar degree, confirming that intentions and action share the same antecedents.
        correct: false
        why: |
          The paper argues the opposite: intentions and action have different drivers, which is why it warns against lumping all non-entrepreneurs into one group.
      - label: "d"
        text: |
          The psychological constructs distinguished those who acted from those who did not, while market-support constructs distinguished intenders from non-intenders.
        correct: true
        why: |
          Market Feasibility and Market Desirability differentiated entrepreneurial action from non-action, whereas the two non-action groups were differentiated by the stakeholder-support constructs (more Bridging-P, less Buffering-I).
    solution: |
      The paper's core empirical message is a division of labour: the marketing-fuelled psychological constructs (**Market Feasibility** and **Market Desirability**) explain who converts intentions into *action*, while stakeholder-support constructs (more Bridging-P, less Buffering-I) explain who forms *intentions* in the first place. This is why the authors insist on treating entrepreneurial propensity as a three-group spectrum rather than a doer/non-doer binary.
    related_terms: [entrepreneurial-propensity, perceived-desirability, stakeholder-support]

    options:

        text: |

      - label: "b"
        text: |
          Because equating "rule-based" with unmodified models is inaccurate, since touchpoint weights can also come from predictive modelling.
        correct: true
        why: |
          The paper says the equalisation between "rule-based" and "not modified methods" is not fully acceptable, so it coins "standardised" (fixed logic) and "custom" (fitted to the dataset) to describe the computational technique separately.
      - label: "c"
        text: |
          Because custom-built models consistently outperform rule-based frameworks and deserved a distinct label.
        correct: false
        why: |
          Contradicts the paper: it explicitly notes custom attribution sometimes performs similarly to simplistic rule-based approaches despite its high expense — superiority is not the motivation.
      - label: "d"
        text: |
          To separate methods that use purely digital data from those that incorporate offline touchpoints.
        correct: false
        why: |
          That is a different taxonomy dimension: the cross-digital vs cross-channel distinction (types of accounted channels), not the computational-technique dimension the new terms describe.
    solution: |
      The taxonomy's five dimensions each capture something different, and the computational-technique dimension had the messiest vocabulary: "rule-based", "algorithmic" and "data-driven" were used interchangeably. Since even rule-based weights can be empirically derived, the authors replaced the muddle with **standardised** (predefined, fixed logic of value allocation) vs **custom** (dynamically modified for a specific marketing context).
