## Measurement, prices & data

### GDP
Gross Domestic Product — the market value of all final goods and services produced domestically. → [[Lec_01-Data Review|Lec 1]]

### GNP
Gross National Product — output produced by a country's residents, wherever located. → [[Lec_01-Data Review|Lec 1]]

### Real vs Nominal
The distinction between price-adjusted (real) and current-price (nominal) values. → [[Lec_01-Data Review|Lec 1]]

### GDP Deflator
A price index based on all domestically-produced goods (nominal ÷ real GDP). → [[Lec_01-Data Review|Lec 1]]

### CPI
Consumer Price Index — the price of a fixed consumption basket. → [[Lec_01-Data Review|Lec 1]]

### Business Cycles
Cyclical fluctuations of output around its trend. → [[Lec_01-Data Review|Lec 1]]

### Fisher Equation
$r \approx i - \pi$ — the real rate is the nominal rate minus inflation. → [[Lec_01-Data Review|Lec 1]]

### Purchasing Power Parity
PPP — exchange rates that equalise the price of a common basket across countries. → [[Lec_01-Data Review|Lec 1]]

### National Accounts
The accounting framework that records output, income, and expenditure. → [[Lec_01-Data Review|Lec 1]]

### Penn World Table
The standard cross-country dataset of comparable real GDP, capital, and productivity used in development and growth accounting. → [[Lec_04-Production|Lec 4]]

### HP Filter
Hodrick–Prescott filter — extracts the smooth trend of a series, leaving the cyclical component. → [[Lec_01-Data Review|Lec 1]]

### Inflation
The rate of change of the general price level. → [[Lec_01-Data Review|Lec 1]]

### Exchange Rates
Nominal and real rates at which currencies trade. → [[Lec_01-Data Review|Lec 1]]

### Fiscal Policy
Government spending and taxation decisions. → [[Lec_10-Fiscal Policy|Lec 10]]

### Monetary Policy
Central-bank interest-rate setting. → [[Lec_01-Data Review|Lec 1]]

### Growth Models
The Solow model and related frameworks for long-run growth. → [[Lec_04-Production|Lec 4]]

## Consumption & saving

### Saving
Income not consumed; in the two-period model, $s = y_1 - c_1$. → [[Lec_02-Consumption and Saving|Lec 2]]

### Euler Equation
The intertemporal optimality condition linking marginal utility across periods: $u'(c_1) = \beta(1+r)\,u'(c_2)$. → [[Lec_02-Consumption and Saving|Lec 2]]

### Life-Cycle Hypothesis
Modigliani: households smooth consumption over the life cycle, saving in working years and dissaving in retirement. → [[Lec_02-Consumption and Saving|Lec 2]]

### Permanent Income Hypothesis
Friedman: consumption depends on permanent (expected lifetime) income, not transitory current income. → [[Lec_02-Consumption and Saving|Lec 2]]

### Intertemporal Choice
The household's allocation of consumption across time subject to a lifetime budget constraint. → [[Lec_02-Consumption and Saving|Lec 2]]

### Fisher Model
The two-period consumption–saving model with borrowing and lending at the real rate $r$. → [[Lec_02-Consumption and Saving|Lec 2]]

### Consumption Smoothing
The preference to keep consumption stable across periods rather than matching the income profile. → [[Lec_02-Consumption and Saving|Lec 2]]

### Lagrangian Optimisation
The method of solving a constrained optimisation (e.g. the consumer's intertemporal problem) via a Lagrangian. → [[Lec_02-Consumption and Saving|Lec 2]]

### Utility Maximisation
The household choosing its consumption path to maximise utility subject to the lifetime budget constraint. → [[Lec_02-Consumption and Saving|Lec 2]]

### Borrowing Constraints
Limits on how much a household can borrow against future income, forcing consumption to track current income. → [[Lec_02-Consumption and Saving|Lec 2]]

### Present Value
The value today of a future payment, discounted at the real interest rate. → [[Lec_02-Consumption and Saving|Lec 2]]

### New Keynesian Models
A class of models with nominal rigidities (sticky prices/wages), referenced here as a contrast to the classical framework. → [[Lec_02-Consumption and Saving|Lec 2]]

## Production & growth

### Production Function
$Y = AF(K,N)$ — output as a function of capital, labor, and productivity. → [[Lec_04-Production|Lec 4]]

### Total Factor Productivity
The $A$ term in the [[Production Function]] — output not explained by measured inputs. → [[Lec_04-Production|Lec 4]]

### Cobb-Douglas
The functional form $Y = AK^\alpha N^\beta$. → [[Lec_04-Production|Lec 4]]

### Constant Returns to Scale
Scaling all inputs by a factor scales output by the same factor (Property 4 of the production function). → [[Lec_04-Production|Lec 4]]

### Marginal Product of Labor
$\text{MPN} = AF_N$ — the extra output from one more unit of labor. → [[Lec_04-Production|Lec 4]]

### Marginal Product of Capital
$\text{MPK} = AF_K$ — the extra output from one more unit of capital. → [[Lec_04-Production|Lec 4]]

### Development Accounting
Decomposing cross-country income differences into inputs vs [[Total Factor Productivity]]. → [[Lec_04-Production|Lec 4]]

### Solow Residual
TFP growth measured as the residual in growth accounting. → [[Lec_04-Production|Lec 4]]

### Real Business Cycles
A theory of fluctuations driven by TFP (productivity) shocks. → [[Lec_04-Production|Lec 4]]

## Investment & capital

### Capital Accumulation Equation
$K_{t+1} = (1-\delta)K_t + I_t$ — next period's capital from depreciation and investment. → [[Lec_05-Investment|Lec 5]]

### User Cost of Capital
The effective cost of using one unit of capital for one period. → [[Lec_05-Investment|Lec 5]]

### Depreciation
The wear-and-tear rate $\delta$ at which capital is used up. → [[Lec_05-Investment|Lec 5]]

### Two-Period Firm Problem
The firm's intertemporal optimisation over investment. → [[Lec_05-Investment|Lec 5]]

## Goods-market equilibrium

### Real Interest Rate
$r$ — the price that clears the goods market. → [[Lec_06-Equilibrium in the Goods Market|Lec 6]]

### Savings-Investment Equilibrium
The $S = I$ condition that clears the goods market. → [[Lec_06-Equilibrium in the Goods Market|Lec 6]]

### Supply Shock
An unexpected change in [[Total Factor Productivity]]. → [[Lec_06-Equilibrium in the Goods Market|Lec 6]]

### Crowding Out
A fiscal expansion raising $r$ and reducing private investment. → [[Lec_06-Equilibrium in the Goods Market|Lec 6]]

### Partial vs General Equilibrium
The distinction between a direct effect and the full market-clearing effect. → [[Lec_06-Equilibrium in the Goods Market|Lec 6]]

## Labor-market data & unemployment

### Unemployment Rate
$u = U/(U+E)$ — the unemployed as a share of the labor force. → [[Lec_08-Labor Market Data, Participation & Unemployment|Lec 8]]

### Labor Force Participation
$(E+U)/$ working-age population. → [[Lec_08-Labor Market Data, Participation & Unemployment|Lec 8]]

### EPOP
The employment-to-population ratio. → [[Lec_08-Labor Market Data, Participation & Unemployment|Lec 8]]

### Sticky Wages
Downward wage rigidity as a source of unemployment. → [[Lec_08-Labor Market Data, Participation & Unemployment|Lec 8]]

### Frictional Unemployment
Unemployment because search and matching take time. → [[Lec_08-Labor Market Data, Participation & Unemployment|Lec 8]]

### Stocks and Flows Model
The two-state E↔U model of the labor market. → [[Lec_08-Labor Market Data, Participation & Unemployment|Lec 8]]

### Job Finding Rate
$f$ — the U-to-E transition probability. → [[Lec_08-Labor Market Data, Participation & Unemployment|Lec 8]]

### Separation Rate
$d$ — the E-to-U transition probability. → [[Lec_08-Labor Market Data, Participation & Unemployment|Lec 8]]

### Search and Matching Model
The DMP model with endogenous job-finding and separation rates. → [[Lec_08-Labor Market Data, Participation & Unemployment|Lec 8]]

### Steady-State Unemployment
The unemployment rate where flows into and out of unemployment balance: $u^* = d/(d+f)$. → [[Lec_08-Labor Market Data, Participation & Unemployment|Lec 8]]

## Fiscal policy

### Government Budget Constraint
$G_t+(1+r)B_{t-1}=T_t+B_t$. → [[Lec_10-Fiscal Policy|Lec 10]]

### Ricardian Equivalence
The equivalence of tax vs debt financing for a given spending path. → [[Lec_10-Fiscal Policy|Lec 10]]

### Lump Sum Tax
A non-distortionary tax — a pure income effect. → [[Lec_10-Fiscal Policy|Lec 10]]

### Labor Income Tax
$\tau_N$ — a distortionary tax that shifts [[Labor Supply]]. → [[Lec_10-Fiscal Policy|Lec 10]]

### Consumption Tax
$\tau_C$ — a distortionary tax on consumption. → [[Lec_10-Fiscal Policy|Lec 10]]

### Public Debt Dynamics
$\Delta b_t = d_t + \frac{r-g}{1+g}b_{t-1}$ — how the debt ratio evolves. → [[Lec_10-Fiscal Policy|Lec 10]]

### Primary Deficit
$D_t = G_t - T_t$ — the deficit excluding interest. → [[Lec_10-Fiscal Policy|Lec 10]]
