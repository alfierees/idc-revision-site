## Asymmetric information & market failure

### Asymmetric Information
One side of a market holds private information the other cannot observe or verify, giving the informed party a strategic advantage. → [[Topic 1 - Asymmetric Information|Topic 1]]

### Adverse Selection
Privately informed agents self-select in ways that harm the uninformed side; high types withdraw and the pool unravels toward a lemons-only equilibrium. → [[Topic 1 - Asymmetric Information|Topic 1]]

### Moral Hazard
A party takes on more risk because it does not bear the full consequences; arises **post-contractually**, unlike adverse selection. → [[Topic 1 - Asymmetric Information|Topic 1]]

### Signaling
An informed party takes a costly action to credibly reveal high quality; works only when the signal is cheaper for high types (separating equilibrium). → [[Topic 1 - Asymmetric Information|Topic 1]]

### Screening
The uninformed party offers a menu of contracts each type self-selects into — the demand-side mirror of [[Signaling]]. → [[Topic 1 - Asymmetric Information|Topic 1]]

### Market Failure
The competitive equilibrium is not Pareto efficient; under [[Asymmetric Information]] the market can collapse to a lemons-only outcome despite gains from trade. → [[Topic 1 - Asymmetric Information|Topic 1]]

### Rational Expectations Equilibrium
A price is consistent with the inferences agents draw from it — the implied pool composition exactly justifies that price. → [[Topic 1 - Asymmetric Information|Topic 1]]

### Common Resource
A rivalrous but non-excludable good; absent coordination each user ignores the externality they impose, causing over-extraction (tragedy of the commons). → [[Topic 1 - Asymmetric Information|Topic 1]]

## Market structures & costs

### Market Structure
The characteristics of a market — number of sellers, product differentiation, entry conditions — that determine how price, quantity, and surplus are split. → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

### Perfect Competition
Many buyers and sellers, a homogeneous product, and free entry; every participant is a **price taker** and the market clears at $P = MC$. → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

### Monopoly
A single seller facing whole-market demand; optimum at $MR = MC$ gives price above MC, output below the competitive level, and deadweight loss. → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

### Oligopoly
A few firms, each aware its choices move the market; equilibrium is the [[Nash Equilibrium]] of the resulting strategic game. → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

### Monopolistic Competition
[[Bertrand Competition]] with differentiated products: each firm has market power on its own demand curve, taking rivals' prices as given. → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

### Differentiated Products
Imperfect substitutes — each firm faces a downward-sloping residual demand even with rivals present. → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

### Cost Functions
$TC(Q)$ splits into explicit/implicit and fixed/variable costs; the two textbook shapes are constant MC ($TC=F+cQ$) and increasing MC (convex $TC$). → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

### Opportunity Cost
The value of the best foregone alternative; counted as an implicit cost, so economic profit is below accounting profit. → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

### Sunk Cost
A cost already incurred and unrecoverable; rational forward-looking decisions ignore it (avoiding the sunk-cost fallacy). → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

### Elasticity
The price elasticity of demand — the responsiveness of quantity to price. → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

### Consumer Surplus
The area between the demand curve and the price paid — the aggregate "deal" consumers get above what they pay. → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

## Monopoly pricing, price discrimination & taxes

### Price Discrimination
Charging different prices for similar goods (not reflecting MC differences) to capture more [[Consumer Surplus]]; comes in three degrees. → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

### First-Degree Price Discrimination
Perfect PD: extract the entire [[Consumer Surplus]] via a [[Two-Part Tariff]] with $p=MC$ and fixed fee $T=CS$. → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

### Second-Degree Price Discrimination
Offer a menu of contracts so consumers self-select by unobserved type; must satisfy [[Incentive Compatibility]]. Also called [[Screening]]. → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

### Third-Degree Price Discrimination
Segment-based pricing on an observable group, with $MR_i = MC$ in each segment. → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

### Two-Part Tariff
A fixed entry fee $T$ plus a per-unit price $p$; the optimum sets $p=MC$ and $T=CS$, the constructive route to perfect PD. → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

### Bundling
Selling goods together at a single price; extracts surplus when consumers' reservation values are negatively correlated. → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

### Constant Elasticity Demand
Demand of the multiplicative form $Q = kP^{-|E|}$ with elasticity constant at every point. → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

### Incentive Compatibility
In a [[Screening]] menu, each type weakly prefers its own contract over any other in the menu. → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

### Information Rent
The surplus a high type keeps because the firm cannot distinguish types and must deter mimicking the low-type contract. → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

### Lerner Index
A markup measure of monopoly power, $L=(P-MC)/P = 1/|E|$. → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

### Per-Unit Tax
A tax on each unit; adds to marginal cost ($MC+t$), raising price (partial pass-through) and lowering quantity. → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

### Lump-Sum Tax
A fixed tax paid whenever $q>0$; does not enter MC, so optimal price and quantity are unchanged. → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

### Tax Incidence
Who economically bears a tax (vs who legally pays). Under linear demand a per-unit tax splits 50/50; the less elastic side bears more. → [[Topic 2 - Equilibrium in Different Market Structures|Topic 2]]

## Game theory

### Game Theory
The formal study of strategic interaction — each player's payoff depends on everyone's actions. → [[Topic 3 - Game Theory|Topic 3]]

### Strategic Interaction
Situations where each player's payoff depends on others' actions, requiring beliefs about what rivals (also rational) will do. → [[Topic 3 - Game Theory|Topic 3]]

### Normal-Form Game
The payoff-matrix representation of a simultaneous-move game; rows are P1's strategies, columns P2's, cells the payoff pairs. → [[Topic 3 - Game Theory|Topic 3]]

### Best Response
The optimal strategy given a specific strategy of the opponent; at a [[Nash Equilibrium]] everyone is playing a best response. → [[Topic 3 - Game Theory|Topic 3]]

### Dominated Strategies
A strategy strictly dominated by another (worse for every opponent action) is never played by a rational player; weak domination relaxes "strictly". → [[Topic 3 - Game Theory|Topic 3]]

### Dominated Strategy
A strategy $s_i$ strictly dominated by $s_i'$ when $s_i'$ yields a strictly higher payoff for every opponent strategy. → [[Topic 3 - Game Theory|Topic 3]]

### Iterated Dominance
IESDS: successively remove strictly [[Dominated Strategy|dominated strategies]]; never eliminates a Nash Equilibrium, order-independent. → [[Topic 3 - Game Theory|Topic 3]]

### Nash Equilibrium
A strategy profile where no player can improve by unilaterally deviating, given everyone else's strategies. → [[Topic 3 - Game Theory|Topic 3]]

### Mixed Strategy
A probability distribution over pure strategies; in equilibrium each player is indifferent among the pure strategies played with positive probability. → [[Topic 3 - Game Theory|Topic 3]]

### Prisoner's Dilemma
Each player has a dominant strategy whose joint play is Pareto-inferior to cooperating — the logic behind cartel collapse. → [[Topic 3 - Game Theory|Topic 3]]

### Coordination Game
Players prefer to coordinate on the same outcome but may disagree which (Battle of the Sexes; risk- vs Pareto-dominant equilibria). → [[Topic 3 - Game Theory|Topic 3]]

### Traveller's Dilemma
Both paid the lower of two named numbers; [[Iterated Dominance]] unravels every value to the floor despite mutual gains from a high value. → [[Topic 3 - Game Theory|Topic 3]]

## Oligopoly competition & complementary goods

### Bertrand Competition
Simultaneous price-setting with homogeneous goods; symmetric costs give $P=MC$ (Bertrand paradox), asymmetric costs give [[Limit Pricing]]. → [[Topic 4 - Price Competition with Complementary Goods|Topic 4]]

### Limit Pricing
Setting price at/just below a rival's MC to deter entry or force out a higher-cost firm. → [[Topic 4 - Price Competition with Complementary Goods|Topic 4]]

### Cournot Competition
Simultaneous quantity-setting; equilibrium is the [[Nash Equilibrium]] where firms' reaction functions cross. → [[Topic 4 - Price Competition with Complementary Goods|Topic 4]]

### Stackelberg Model
Sequential quantity choice; the leader substitutes the follower's reaction function into its own profit for a first-mover advantage. → [[Topic 4 - Price Competition with Complementary Goods|Topic 4]]

### Cartel
Firms coordinate output to maximise joint profit (act as a monopolist); jointly optimal but unstable, like a [[Prisoner's Dilemma]]. → [[Topic 4 - Price Competition with Complementary Goods|Topic 4]]

### Perfect Complements
Goods that must be consumed together with no partial consumption; the consumer cares only about the total bundle price. → [[Topic 4 - Price Competition with Complementary Goods|Topic 4]]

### Double Marginalization
Each firm over a [[Perfect Complements|complementary]] component marks up independently, stacking markups and raising the final price. → [[Topic 4 - Price Competition with Complementary Goods|Topic 4]]

### Pricing Externality
The harm one firm's price hike imposes on rivals' revenues by reducing total demand in a complementary-goods market. → [[Topic 4 - Price Competition with Complementary Goods|Topic 4]]

### Vertical Integration
Merging complementary/upstream-downstream firms to maximise joint profit on the total price, internalising the [[Pricing Externality]] and reversing [[Double Marginalization]]. → [[Topic 4 - Price Competition with Complementary Goods|Topic 4]]

### Vertical Relations
An upstream producer sells to a downstream retailer, each marking up; independent optimisation yields [[Double Marginalization]]. → [[Topic 4 - Price Competition with Complementary Goods|Topic 4]]

### Complementary Monopolist
A firm with monopoly power over one component of a complementary bundle, pricing it while taking the others' prices as given. → [[Topic 4 - Price Competition with Complementary Goods|Topic 4]]

### Best Response Function
A firm's profit-maximising own strategy as a function of the rival's; its intersection with the rival's is the [[Nash Equilibrium]]. → [[Topic 4 - Price Competition with Complementary Goods|Topic 4]]

### Comparative Statics
How equilibrium outcomes change as a parameter (most often the number of firms $N$) changes. → [[Topic 4 - Price Competition with Complementary Goods|Topic 4]]
