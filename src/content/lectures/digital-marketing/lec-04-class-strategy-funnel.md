## TL;DR
The lecture builds one idea: a campaign is a **strategy**, and a strategy is a route on a map. It is defined by **three questions** — *Where are we now? Where do we want to be? How do we get there?* **"Where are we now?"** audits four things (Competitors, Clients, Communication environment, Company). **"Where do we want to be?"** cascades from a **Vision/Mission statement → Destinations → [[KPI|KPIs]] → Conversion**, with a defined menu of possible goals (attention, brand awareness, reputation, leads, behaviour shift, orders/sales…). The **marketing funnel** (Awareness → Engagement → Persuasion → Action → Advocate) maps those goals onto three phases — **Traffic → Attention → Conversion** — and **conversion is whatever the strategy says it is** (from "watch a video" to "purchase"). **"How do we get there?"** is the execution checklist: message, channels, target population, conversion definition, measurements — run as a **Traffic → Engage → Convert** loop of *Measure → Analyze → Regenerate*.

# Part 1 — Strategy: the three defining questions

## Strategy as a route on a map
> [!quote] The Cheshire Cat (Alice in Wonderland), on the "what is strategy?" slide
> Alice: *Which way should I go?*
> Cat: *That depends on where you are going.*
> Alice: *I don't know where I'm going.*
> Cat: *Then it doesn't matter which way you go!*

The teaching point (the slide pairs this with a **treasure-map** graphic): **a strategy only exists once you know your destination.** Without a defined goal, no channel, message or budget choice can be "right" — every road is as good as any other. This is the frame for the whole lecture.

## The three questions
> [!example] STRATEGY — 3 defining questions (verbatim from the slide)
> 1. **WHERE are we now?**
> 2. **WHERE do we want to be?**
> 3. **HOW do we get there?**

The rest of the lecture is one question per section.

# Part 2 — "Where are we now?" (the situation audit)

Before setting a goal, audit the current situation across **four C-like domains** (reproduced from the slide):

> [!example] Where we are now — the four-quadrant audit
> | Domain | What to map |
> |---|---|
> | **Competitors** | Alternative suppliers; alternative or current norms & behaviours (what people do *instead* of buying you) |
> | **Clients** | Target audiences; influencers; **sales model** — [[B2B / B2C\|B2B? B2C? B2B2C?]] |
> | **Communication** | Current public atmosphere; current media environment |
> | **Company** | Management; employees; stakeholders |

The logic: **"now" is not just your product** — it's the competitive alternatives, who your buyers and their influencers are, the *mood* of the public conversation you're stepping into, and your own internal reality (leadership, staff, stakeholders). Note that **Competitors** includes *current norms and behaviours* — sometimes your real rival is "the customer does nothing / keeps their old habit," not another brand.

# Part 3 — "Where do we want to be?" (from vision to KPIs)

## The vision-to-KPI cascade
> [!example] From vision to KPIs (top-down, verbatim from the slide)
> **Vision / [[Mission Statement]]** → **Destinations** → **[[KPI|KPIs]]** → **CONVERSION**

Read it as a funnel of specificity. A broad **[[Vision Statement|vision / mission]]** (who we are, why we exist) is broken into concrete **destinations** (where this campaign should land the brand), which are made measurable as **[[KPI|Key Performance Indicators]]**, which finally resolve into a single defined **conversion** — the specific action that counts as success. Each level makes the one above it *operational*: a mission you can't measure is a slogan.

## The defined-goals / KPI menu
Not every campaign chases a sale. The deck lists the **menu of goals a campaign can pursue**, any of which can be the KPI:

> [!example] Defined Goals / KPI (verbatim from the slide, both columns)
> - **Attention**
> - **Brand awareness**
> - **Reputation management**
> - **Leads** *(see [[Lead Generation]])*
> - **Behaviour shift** *(see [[Behaviour Change]])*
> - **Information requests**
> - **Orders**
> - **Sales / Voting**
> - **Opinion setting**
> - **Stock-price change**
> - **Regulatory shift**

> [!tip] Exam-relevant reading of this list
> The list runs, roughly, **top-of-funnel → bottom-of-funnel**: attention and awareness sit at the wide end; orders, sales and votes at the narrow end; and there are **non-commercial** goals (opinion setting, regulatory shift, stock-price change) that a purely "sales" view of marketing would miss. Picking the *wrong* KPI for your funnel stage is a classic trap — an awareness campaign judged on immediate sales looks like a failure when it wasn't one.

# Part 4 — The Marketing Funnel

*The funnel below is reproduced from the deck's funnel diagram; the phase mapping (Traffic / Attention / Conversion) is printed on the slide.*

> [!example] The Marketing Funnel — stages ↔ phases
> | Funnel stage (top → bottom) | Phase |
> |---|---|
> | **Awareness** | **TRAFFIC** (top) / **ATTENTION** |
> | **Engagement** | **ATTENTION** |
> | **Persuasion** | **ATTENTION → CONVERSION** |
> | **Action** | **CONVERSION** |
> | **Advocate** | **CONVERSION** |

The stages are the customer's psychological journey — from first **Awareness**, through **Engagement** (they interact), **Persuasion** (they're convinced), **Action** (they do the thing), to **Advocate** (they tell others, feeding fresh awareness back to the top). The three **phases** group those stages by *what the marketer is buying*:

- **Traffic** — getting eyeballs onto the content at all (top of funnel).
- **Attention** — holding and deepening that interest (awareness → engagement → persuasion).
- **Conversion** — turning attention into the defined action, and beyond it into advocacy.

*Reconstructed link to Lecture 2's metrics:* this is the same top-to-bottom logic as the CPM (traffic/eyeballs) → CPC (traffic) → CPA (action) split — see [[Digital Marketing Lecture 2]]. The funnel is why "which metric is best? it depends" — it depends on which phase you're paying for.

## What defines *conversion*?
The pivotal idea of the lecture: **conversion is not automatically "a purchase."** A slide titled *"Conversion? — according to the strategy"* makes the point explicit — conversion is **whatever action your strategy declared the goal**. The deck lists example conversions (verbatim):

> [!example] What can count as a conversion (verbatim list)
> - **Listen / watch / read**
> - **React / comment / share**
> - **Provide contact info / register**
> - **Provide FFF contact info** *(friends, family & followers — referrals)*
> - **Sign up / create an account**
> - **Download app / software / Purchase / Apply**
> - **Ask for further details**
> - **Vote / avoid voting**
> - **Take action / take no action**
> - **Change your mind / don't change your mind**
> - **…etc.**

> [!tip] Why this matters for the exam
> Notice conversions can be **negative or passive** — *avoid voting*, *take no action*, *don't change your mind*. If your strategic goal is to *stop* people doing something, the "conversion" is the non-action. This is exactly why the funnel and KPI must be defined **before** you measure: the same campaign is a triumph or a flop depending on which of these you declared the conversion.

## The Traffic → Engage → Convert loop
*Reconstructed from the "Generate Traffic → Engage → Convert" diagram with a light-trails motorway image.*

> [!example] The optimisation loop (verbatim labels)
> **Generate Traffic → Engage → Convert**
> underpinned by: **Measure ↔ Analyze ↔ Regenerate**

The top row is the funnel compressed into three verbs; the bottom row is the **feedback engine** that runs beneath it. You don't fire a campaign once — you **Measure** what happened, **Analyze** why, and **Regenerate** (revise creative, targeting, budget) to feed the next cycle. It's the operational, always-on version of the funnel: attention and conversion are continuously re-earned, not banked.

# Part 5 — "How do we get there?" (the execution checklist)

Once the destination (KPI) and the conversion are set, the *how* is a five-item build order:

> [!example] How we get there (verbatim from the slide, in order)
> 1. **Message / Story formulation** — what you actually say *(storytelling craft is owned by [[Digital Marketing Lecture 5]])*
> 2. **Channels / Platforms** — where you say it *(the platform landscape is owned by [[Digital Marketing Lecture 2]])*
> 3. **Target population definition** — who you say it to *(personas & segmentation owned by [[Digital Marketing Lecture 3]])*
> 4. **Conversion definition** — what counts as success *(owned by this note, above)*
> 5. **Measurements** — how you'll know *(the [[KPI|KPIs]] from Part 3; measurement depth owned by [[Digital Marketing Lecture 10]])*

The order is deliberate: **message before channel** (you decide the story, then pick the medium that carries it — "the medium is the message"), and **conversion + measurement last** so that what you measure is locked to the goal you set in Part 3. This checklist is essentially the three strategic questions turned into a to-do list.

# Modern aside — AI persona generation

*Reconstructed from the ChatGPT / Gemini persona slides; the generated text is reproduced from the deck.*

To illustrate persona-building (owned by [[Digital Marketing Lecture 3]]), the deck runs a live demo: ask **ChatGPT** and **Gemini Pro** to generate customer personas for **Diet Coke vs Coke Zero**. The tools produce plausible, well-structured profiles — ChatGPT's "Diana, 35, Marketing Manager, yoga, health-conscious" for Diet Coke and "Alex, 35, Software Engineer, gaming, tech" for Coke Zero; Gemini's sharper "Diet Coke *Ritualist*" (aesthetic, ritual, the 3 PM slump reset) vs "Coke Zero *Optimizer*" (performance, bio-hacking, the reward moment).

> [!warning] "ChatGPT? Not so fast…" — the slide's own caveat
> The deck flags the limitation directly with a *"Not so fast…"* slide that sets the two ChatGPT personas side by side. The problem: the outputs are **near-identical templates with the demographics swapped** — same age (35), same "health-conscious but busy," same "concerned about the environment," same "convenient healthy drink" pain point. AI persona generation is a fast **starting point**, but it regresses to a generic mean and **invents specifics that sound confident without evidence**. It does not replace real customer discovery — use it to draft, then validate against actual data. *(General-AI capabilities and limits: [[Generative AI]].)*

## Exam takeaways
> [!tip] What this lecture sets up for the past papers
> - **The three questions** are the skeleton of any campaign answer: *Where now? → Where to? → How?*
> - **"Where now?"** = Competitors / Clients / Communication / Company (and "competitor" can mean the customer's *current behaviour*).
> - **The cascade:** Vision/Mission → Destinations → **KPIs** → Conversion. A goal you can't measure isn't a KPI.
> - **KPIs are a menu**, not just "sales" — attention, awareness, reputation, leads, behaviour shift, orders, votes, opinion, regulation.
> - **The funnel:** Awareness → Engagement → Persuasion → Action → Advocate, grouped as **Traffic → Attention → Conversion**.
> - **Conversion = whatever the strategy defines it as** — including *non*-actions. Define it *before* you measure.
> - **Loop:** Traffic → Engage → Convert, run continuously as Measure → Analyze → Regenerate.
> - **KPIs and funnel logic appear in past papers** — see [[2024 Exam B1]].
