# Sharing Consumers' Brand Storytelling: Influence of Consumers' Storytelling on Brand Attitude via Emotions and Cognitions

> Part of: [[Digital Marketing]]
> **Topic 3 — Formation of Campaign Strategy** · Reading
> Citation: Hong, J. (Jenny), Yang, J., Wooldridge, B. R., & Bhappu, A. D. (2022). Sharing consumers' brand storytelling: influence of consumers' storytelling on brand attitude via emotions and cognitions. *Journal of Product & Brand Management, 31*(2), 265–278.
> Key concepts: [[Brand Storytelling]], [[Brand Attitude]], [[Narrative Transportation]], [[Word of Mouth]], [[Consumer-Generated Content]], [[Self-Referencing]]

---

## TL;DR
Brand storytelling builds strong brands — but does a story told *by other consumers* work better than one told *by the firm*? Across three experiments the authors show that **consumers' brand storytelling** produces **more favourable brand attitudes** than a firm-created [[Brand Story]]. The mechanism is a **dual mediation**: consumer storytelling triggers both deeper **cognitive processing** and stronger **positive emotions**, which together drive the more favourable attitude. Consumer storytelling is also rated as more authentic and more shareable.

## Why It's on the Reading List
For **Topic 3 (Formation of Campaign Strategy)**, it teaches *where* persuasive narrative power comes from in a campaign: encouraging and amplifying consumer-told stories (UGC) rather than relying only on polished firm narratives. It supplies the cognition + emotion mechanism and the conceptual distinction between brand story, WoM and consumer brand storytelling.

## Background & Research Question
Stories "move people." [[Brand Storytelling]] — a compelling story creating emotional connections between brand and consumer, with plot, actors, causality and chronology — is an effective branding tool. But two sources exist:

- **Brand story** = **firm-created**, controlled, one-to-many (ads, packaging, founder/CEO stories). Consumers are *passive recipients*; they may discount it as a sales tactic.
- **Consumers' brand storytelling** = **created, elaborated and shared by consumers** from their own experience; a narrative form of [[Word of Mouth]] (but distinct from WoM because it has narrative structure — chronology and causality).

**Research question:** Does consumers' brand storytelling raise brand attitudes more than a firm brand story, and *why* (through cognition and/or emotion)?

## Key Concepts & Definitions

> [!info] Definition — Consumers' brand storytelling
> A narrative format of a consumption experience, *created and shared by consumers*. A distinct sub-category under the umbrella of [[Word of Mouth]] — WoM is broader and need not be structured as a story.

> [!info] Definition — Narrative transportation
> "A convergent process, where all mental systems and capabilities become focused on events occurring in the narrative" (Green & Brock, 2000). Stories transport audiences into the events.

> [!info] Definition — Self-referencing
> Processing information by relating it to oneself or one's own experiences. Consumer stories are similar to the reader (peer reference group), so they trigger more self-referencing and familiarity.

> [!note] Cognition vs emotion (Chaudhuri, 2006)
> **Cognition** = sequential, analytical processing of product information (costs/benefits/attributes). **Emotion** = holistic, synthetic integration of sensory data. Brand attitude is a function of *both* the rational and emotional evaluation.

## Main Arguments / Findings

The hypotheses:

| H | Statement |
|---|---|
| H1 | Consumer storytelling → **higher cognitive processing** than a brand story |
| H2 | Consumer storytelling → **greater positive emotions** than a brand story |
| H3 | Consumer storytelling → **more favourable brand attitude** than a brand story |
| H4 | The storytelling → attitude effect is **mediated** by higher cognition *and* greater positive emotions |

**Study 1 results** (n = 200; coffee shop & airline-mileage brands):

- Cognitive processing higher for consumer storytelling (M = 2.8 vs 2.1) — supports **H1**.
- Positive emotions higher (M = 4.8 vs 4.2) — supports **H2**.
- Brand attitude higher (M = 5.2 vs 4.6) — supports **H3**.
- Parallel mediation (PROCESS Model 4): both cognition and positive emotions significantly mediate → supports **H4**. No interaction with product category.

**Study 2** (n = 117; different measures — *cognitive personalisation* and a 7-item positive-emotion scale; controls for prior experience and involvement) **replicated** the effects and added two confirmations: consumer storytelling drives **higher motivation to share** (M = 4.7 vs 3.5) and **greater authenticity** (M = 5.4 vs 4.3). Mediation through cognition and emotion held.

> [!example] Why consumer stories win
> Because the source is *similar to the reader*, consumers feel more familiarity and favourability, engage in more **self-referencing** and **narrative transportation**, see the message as **less biased**, and experience stronger emotions from "real" rather than "artificial" stories. Firm stories, once recognised as a sales tactic, can trigger correction/reverse behaviour (e.g. discounting Walmart's "Save Money. Live Better").

## Methodology
Three between-subjects experiments. Core design: **2 (story: consumers' brand storytelling vs firm brand story) × 2 (product: coffee shop vs airline mileage)**.

- Real-but-unfamiliar brands (Angel-in-us coffee; Emirates Skywards) used so prior experience ≈ zero, removing brand bias; only positive valence studied.
- Manipulation checks confirmed perceived source (consumer vs company).
- Brand attitude measured on 4 semantic-differential items; positive emotions and cognitive processing/personalisation measured separately.
- Mediation via Hayes PROCESS Model 4 (10,000 bootstrap samples). Prior experience and involvement used as controls in Studies 2–3.

## Framework / Model

Dual-mediation model:

**Consumers' brand storytelling → ↑ Cognitive processing → ↑ Brand attitude**
**Consumers' brand storytelling → ↑ Positive emotions → ↑ Brand attitude**

(vs a firm-created brand story as the comparison condition.)

## Implications for Marketers
- **Encourage loyal customers to tell their own stories** and give them comfortable platforms (social media, brand communities) to do so.
- Consumer storytelling on social media is more persuasive than traditional firm channels and drives more sharing — capitalise on the UGC trend.
- Applications beyond products: **internal branding** (employees prefer fellow-employee stories to management narratives), **tourism/hospitality** (guest reviews/experiences), and **destination branding** (co-creation by visitors).
- Brand managers can no longer ignore consumer voices; treat consumers as **co-creators** of the brand.

## Exam Takeaways
> [!tip] Likely exam points
> - Three constructs: **brand story** (firm, controlled, one-to-many) vs **consumers' brand storytelling** (consumer-created narrative) vs **WoM** (broader, not necessarily narrative).
> - Consumer storytelling beats firm story on **brand attitude**.
> - Dual mechanism (H4): mediation by **cognitive processing AND positive emotions**.
> - Why it works: similarity → self-referencing, narrative transportation, perceived authenticity, less perceived bias, higher motivation to share.
> - Method: 2×2 between-subjects experiments + PROCESS mediation.

## Summary
- Consumer-told brand stories outperform firm-told ones on brand attitude.
- The effect runs through both deeper cognition and stronger positive emotion.
- Consumer storytelling is more authentic and more shareable.
- Strategic lesson: facilitate and amplify consumer storytelling as a campaign tool.
