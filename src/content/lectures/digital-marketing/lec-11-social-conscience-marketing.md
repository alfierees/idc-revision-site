# BrandFusion Ch. 3.13 — Social Conscience: Social Marketing

> Part of: [[Digital Marketing]]

## TL;DR
This chapter argues "the meaning of marketing is the marketing of meaning" — brand strategy is increasingly **socially driven**, with value shifting from shareholder to social value. The focal section, §3.13, defines **social marketing** as "the systematic application of marketing concepts and techniques to achieve specific behavioural goals, for a social or public good". Its essence is **behaviour change** — operating *upstream* (policy), *midstream* (community partners) or *downstream* (individual). The section sets out six core social-marketing concepts and locates "social conscience" as one of eight elements in the chapter's **Social-Dominant Logic** ecosystem. Surrounding cases (Dove, Always #LikeAGirl, Libresse #BloodNormal, TOMS, Absolut) illustrate **purpose-driven branding**.

## Why It's on the Reading List
It is the conceptual anchor for the social/public-campaigns and CSR topic: it gives a precise definition of social marketing, its six core concepts, and the upstream/midstream/downstream model of behaviour change, while connecting purpose-driven branding to broader social-dominant logic. The campaign cases are ready-made exam illustrations.

## Background & Research Question
The chapter situates marketing within a **social context** (Granovetter, 1985) — economic exchange never happens in a vacuum. It argues that consumers now favour purpose: per the opening case, **63% of consumers prefer purpose-driven brands** and **81% of millennials expect declarations of corporate citizenship**. "There is no brand purpose if there is no brand meaning" — and no meaning without value(s). The aim is to present a **Social-Dominant Logic** as a "parallax perspective" (not a paradigm shift) building on product-, service-, customer- and consumer-dominant logics.

## Key Concepts & Definitions

> [!info] Definition — Social Marketing (National Social Marketing Centre, 2006)
> "The systematic application of marketing concepts and techniques to achieve specific behavioural goals, for a social or public good." Focused on creating social value for individuals, communities and whole societies (iSMA, 2021).

> [!info] Definition — Social Marketing (iSMA, 2021)
> Seeks to develop and integrate marketing concepts with other approaches to influence behaviours that benefit individuals and communities for the greater social good. Guided by ethical principles; integrates research, best practice, theory, audience and partnership insight to deliver social-change programmes that are *effective, efficient, equitable and sustainable*.

> [!info] Definition — Levels of behaviour-change intervention
> **Upstream** — policy change (e.g. limiting fast-food portions, supplying healthy alternatives). **Midstream** — working with partners/groups in co-creative community initiatives. **Downstream** — individual behavioural change. "The core is all about behaviour."

> [!info] Definition — Purpose-driven branding
> Brand (and company) purpose that is socially driven — standing *with* customers to stand out from competitors, affecting real social change rather than merely selling product.

## Main Arguments / Findings

### The six core Social Marketing concepts

| # | Concept | Essence |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Explicit social goals** | Set with equally explicit *metrics* enabling ongoing measurement/evaluation (strategic goals + behavioural objectives) |
| 2 | **Citizen orientation & focus** | Engage communities/beneficiaries in selecting, developing, delivering and evaluating programmes — not experts alone deciding |
| 3 | **Value propositions via the Intervention Mix** | Deliver social good through a *mix* (services, social products, policy, environments, incentives, sanctions, recommended behaviours) built on audience insight |
| 4 | **Theory/insight/data-informed segmentation** | Cluster people by shared beliefs, attitudes, behaviours and social pressures for customised support |
| 5 | **Competition, barrier & asset analysis** | Identify and reduce the "competition" encouraging negative behaviour (significant others, media, environment, culture, economics, social norms); build implementation coalitions; scope community assets |
| 6 | **Critical thinking, reflexivity & ethical practice** | Systematic + systemic; uses theory, reflexivity, cultural acceptability, and ethics (acceptability, transparency, balancing benefits vs costs) |

> [!example] Social forces drive social marketing
> Norms, influence, obedience, compliance, conformity, self-image and peer affiliation are vital. Role models/influencers help ideas diffuse and reach a **"tipping point"** (Gladwell, 2000) — e.g. Black Lives Matter. Opinion leaders can also create *resistance* — e.g. the "Anti-Vaxer" contingent hampering COVID-19 vaccine roll-out. Counter-consumption goals (anti-tobacco) are sometimes called **"anti-marketing"** — Hastings & Saren (2003) call this a "symbiotic relationship between social, commercial and critical marketing thought".

### Purpose-driven brand cases (opening case + §3.14)

> [!example] Campaign illustrations
> - **L'Oréal "Because You're Worth It"** (1971) — self-worth positioning; later "Create the Beauty That Moves The World" (2021).
> - **Dove "Campaign for Real Beauty" / #ShowUs** — challenged beauty stereotypes; ~$4bn sales boost; 5,000+ images across 39 countries; 2022 UNICEF partnership.
> - **P&G Always #LikeAGirl / Unstoppable** — turned an insult into empowerment; 90m views, 12bn impressions; education hosted on TED-Ed (issue *awareness* → audience *education*).
> - **Essity Libresse/Bodyform #BloodNormal & "Womb Stories"** — purpose: "breaking taboos that hold women back" (taboos must trigger some negativity or they wouldn't be taboos).
> - **TOMS Shoes** — one-for-one model; mission → strategy → narrative; 50m+ free pairs, extended to sight, water and safe-birth programmes.
> - **Absolut "Drink Responsibly, #SexResponsibly"** — provocateur brand starting a *consent* conversation (post-#MeToo); sponsors RAINN.

## Framework / Model — the Social-Dominant Logic ecosystem

§3.13 ("Social Conscience") is element **6** of an eight-part ecosystem that synthesises customer-oriented marketing perspectives into a socially-driven brand strategy.

| # | Ecosystem element | Key component | Key concept |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Social value-in-use | Service marketing | Co-creation in context |
| 2 | Social salience | Experience marketing | Symbolic meaning |
| 3 | Social semiotics | Universal language of branding | Experiential branding |
| 4 | Social media | Digitalised communications | C2C, B2C communications |
| 5 | Social interaction | Brand communities | Social linking value |
| **6** | **Social conscience** | **Societal marketing** | **Purpose, CSR** |
| 7 | Social stories | Brand equity | Brand narratives |
| 8 | Social partnerships | Relationship marketing & dialogue | Social relationships |

Social-Dominant Logic implies: shared socially-constituted meaning; co-creation of value; social subjectivity of agency; symmetry in the customer–company relationship; and social communal influence forming loyalty.

## Implications for Marketers

- "Stand with your customers to stand out from your competitors" — but purpose must be **authentic and metric-backed**, not bandwagon-jumping (the opening case explicitly questions whether femvertising is empowerment or "cynical surfing").
- Move campaigns from **issue awareness → audience education** (Always/TED-Ed) for durable brand purpose.
- Behaviour change needs targeted **segmentation** and **competition/barrier analysis** — identify who encourages the *un*wanted behaviour.
- **Stakeholder capitalism**: Millennials/Gen Z hold brands accountable for societal impact; brands often have "no choice" but to align with customers' social causes.
