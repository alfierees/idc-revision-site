## TL;DR
The lecture bridges from **brand** into **content**: the emotional pull of a brand (attachment) only pays off if the brand stays **relevant in the moments that matter**, and content is how you stay relevant. It defines **content marketing** (Damian Ryan), argues *why it works* (low barriers to entry, engagement over interruption), and sets a value bar with the **EEAT** framework (content must be **relevant, personalized, needs-oriented**). It then maps **content to the 6-stage Customer Journey** (Awareness → Consideration → Decision → Onboarding → Retention → Win Back) and gives three planning lenses: the **Content Strategy Matrix** (Entertain/Inspire/Educate/Convince), Google's **3H model** (Hero/Hub/Help) and **Content Properties**. It closes on calendars/tools, content **trends**, **AI's limits and opportunities**, and an **influencer** intro (Doggy Vacay Tour).

# Part 0 — The bridge: brand → content

## Recap (owned elsewhere — cross-link only)
The deck reopens the brand material from the previous session: **Earned media** (the right medium enhances the story and meets the audience in the right time and place), the **3V's model** (Virtues, Values, Voice = Brand Identity), brand **storytelling archetypes**, **Scott Galloway's** body model (Google = brain, Facebook = heart, Amazon = gut, Apple = genitals/lust), and the **Peloton** relevance case. All of this is owned by → [[Digital Marketing Lecture 5]]; personas & touchpoints that reappear on the recap slides are owned by → [[Digital Marketing Lecture 3]]; the marketing funnel/KPI recap is owned by → [[Digital Marketing Lecture 4]]. This note does **not** redefine them.

## Brand attachment vs brand relevance (the one piece this note owns)
The recap ends on one distinction that is the actual hinge into content:

> [!example] Attachment ≠ relevance
> - **Brand attachment** = *"the emotional connection between humans and brands"* (Joann Sciarinno). It's the feeling — the reason someone shaves the Apple logo into the back of their head.
> - **Brand relevance** = being useful *in the moments that matter most to consumers*. Scott Davis (Prophet, 2017): brands must *"create a product that people love enough to integrate into their everyday lives… the brands that inspire this level of loyalty will grow the fastest because they are relevant in the moments that matter."*

The teaching point: attachment is the emotional *pull*, but it decays if the brand isn't **relevant** — present and useful when the customer actually has a need. The nine "relentlessly relevant" brands (Apple, Google, Bose, Spotify, Toyota, Peloton, KitchenAid, Android, USAA) stay top-decile by *meeting the moment*. **Content marketing is the mechanism for staying relevant** — showing up with something valuable at each moment of the journey. That is the whole reason the lecture pivots from brand to content.

# Part 1 — Content marketing: what and why

## Definition
> [!quote] Damian Ryan (2014)
> **Content marketing is any communication by a company or organization that is so interesting the target group uses it *voluntarily*.** By contrast, traditional classical marketing *interrupts* desired content with *undesired* content.

The cartoon on the slide makes the contrast literal: content marketing is the sandcastle *inside* the fishbowl that the cat wants to watch; traditional advertising is the "Worm Wiz-Bang" pop-up shoved in front of the worms. *"Content is the currency of influence"* (Traackr) — you earn attention rather than buying an interruption.

## Why it works
> [!tip] Two arguments from the Damian Ryan slides
> 1. **It's not new, but the digital age amplified it — it lowers the barriers of entry for content creators and is cost-effective.** Anyone can publish; you no longer need to buy a TV slot or a print page. *(This "levelling the playing field" idea is exam-relevant — see takeaways.)*
> 2. **It offers value to potential customers: engagement instead of trying to sell.** You give something useful/entertaining first; the sale is a downstream consequence of the relationship.

A further Damian Ryan quote frames the reach: *"content is one of the few marketing channels that allows marketers to contact their potential customers along **all phases** of the customer cycle"* — Discover → Purchase → Bill → Pay → Use → Customer Service → Retention. That "all phases" claim is exactly what Part 2 unpacks. The deck also nods to Greg Isenberg's *"ditch the funnel, create an orbit"* — no purchase is truly linear, customers **orbit** a brand while they decide, which is why content must be everywhere in the cycle rather than a single funnel push.

# Part 2 — What makes content *valuable*: EEAT

Not all content is worth consuming. The deck sets the bar in two layers.

## The value test: relevant · personalized · needs-oriented
The headline rule (the "provide VALUE to the customer" slide): valuable content is **Relevant**, **Personalized**, and **Needs-oriented**. If a piece isn't at least one of these for the persona in front of it, it's clutter. (Persona is the input — a "REMINDER" slide points back to buyer personas from → [[Digital Marketing Lecture 3]].)

## The EEAT framework
> [!example] EEAT — the four pillars of content value (HubSpot 2025)
> | Pillar | On the slide |
> |---|---|
> | **E — Expertise** | The skill or knowledge in a particular field. |
> | **E — Experience** | The practical knowledge gained through involvement in events or activities. |
> | **A — Authority** | The quality of being reliable and respected. |
> | **T — Trustworthiness** | The quality of being reliable and honest. |

EEAT originates in Google's search-quality guidance and is used here as a **content-quality rubric**: content that demonstrably shows expertise, first-hand experience, authority and trust is what earns both algorithmic ranking and human belief. *The mapping of each pillar to its one-line gloss is verbatim from the slide; the "why it ranks" framing is reconstructed standard SEO/content theory.*

## Content formats library
Before strategy, the deck inventories the raw material — the **formats** content can take. Two "classic formats" slides plus two dense "content marketing plan: formats" slides give a large library:

> [!example] Content formats (from the deck's format slides)
> **Classic 8:** White papers · E-books · Blogs/Vlogs · Newsletters · Infographics · Music · Give-aways · Texts/mobile.
> **Extended library:** How-to's · Content curation · Case studies · Charts/graphs · Email newsletters/autoresponders · Cartoons/illustrations · Book summaries · Tool reviews · Giveaways · Helpful app/tool · Opinion posts · White papers · Videos · Templates · Surveys · SlideShares · Quotes · FAQs · Q&A sessions · Webinars · Guides · Dictionary · "Day in the life" posts · Infographics · Interviews · Lists · Mind maps · Memes · Online games · Quizzes · Polls · Podcasts · Pinboards · Photo collages · Original research · Press releases · Photos · Predictions · **[[UGC|User-Generated Content]]** · Company news · Timelines.
> The one instruction stapled to the whole library: every format must **provide VALUE to the customer.**

*(Worked format examples in the deck: Barilla's Spotify pasta-timing playlists, Spotify "Sound of Colleagues", BuzzFeed quizzes/lists, the MIT Moral Machine — all illustrating "content people use voluntarily.")*

# Part 3 — Content along the Customer Journey

This is a core owned section. The deck presents a **B2B-oriented, 6-stage [[Customer Journey]]** split into **pre-conversion** and **post-conversion**, and — for each stage — lists the **[[Touchpoint|touchpoints]]** where you meet the customer and the **content** you serve them. (Source on the slides: *The Customer Journey Handbook*, Uberflip 2021. This is a fuller, service/B2B version of the [[Customer Decision Journey]] and marketing-funnel ideas from → [[Digital Marketing Lecture 4]].)

> [!example] The 6 stages
> **Pre-conversion:** 1 Awareness → 2 Consideration → 3 Decision
> **Post-conversion:** 4 Onboarding → 5 Retention → 6 Win Back

| # | Stage | Customer mindset | Signature touchpoints | Content to serve |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Awareness** | Doesn't know you yet; look to your **buyer personas** and their goals/challenges | Search engines, paid media, website, resource centre, chatbot, social, newswire, events | Guides/reports, press releases, influencer & guest posts, educational content (blogs, videos, podcasts, webinars, infographics) |
| 2 | **Consideration** | Goal defined, now in **buy mode**, doing intensive independent research | Paid media (**retargeting**), email, search, landing pages, reviews/forums, community | Webinars, demo & product videos, expert guides, **comparison whitepapers**, customer stories, middle-of-funnel content |
| 3 | **Decision** | Decided they need *a* solution — the **pros-and-cons list** stage; *"arguably the most important from a content perspective"* | Email, phone, website, sales & campaign destinations, reviews/forums, chatbot | Customer stories, **vendor comparisons**, sales presentations, live demos, product videos, product collateral, the **sales contract** |
| 4 | **Onboarding** | Has bought; wants to **get up and running** with minimal friction | Product itself, email, phone, knowledge base, customer-education centre | Knowledge-base docs, quick-start guides/videos, product tutorials, training decks, transactional emails, in-app messages, direct-mail welcome packages |
| 5 | **Retention** | The window between go-live and renewal; keep delivering on the promise → may become a **brand advocate** | Product, knowledge base, education centre, email, phone, events, advocate community | Best-practice guides, customer emails/newsletters, customer spotlights, success plans, maturity assessments, **gamification** content, training decks |
| 6 | **Win Back** | Hasn't found value; wants **out** of the contract/subscription | Email, phone, website, product, events, chatbot, social | Re-engagement nurture, educational resources, success plans, **exit surveys**, product updates, direct-mail packages |

> [!tip] The exam-shaped idea
> **The stage dictates the content.** Awareness/Consideration content is *acquisition* (educate, build trust before the sale); Retention/Win-Back content is *nurture* aimed at the **penetrated market** — existing customers — which is the retention-oriented use of content marketing tested in the past papers. Real-world stage examples on the slides: Home Depot's free workshops and an LG microwave manual as *Onboarding* content.

# Part 4 — Three content-strategy frameworks

The deck labels three planning lenses and stresses they answer *different* questions:

> [!example] The three strategies
> 1. **Content Strategy Matrix** — organise content **by customer stage** (emotion vs rationale × awareness vs purchase).
> 2. **Google's 3H model** — organise **by content usage / cadence** (Hero / Hub / Help).
> 3. **Content Properties** — organise **by attributes** (date, audience persona, format, channel, etc.).

## (a) The Content Strategy Matrix — Entertain / Inspire / Educate / Convince
A 2×2 on two axes: **Emotional ↔ Rational** (vertical) and **Awareness ↔ Purchase** (horizontal). Each quadrant is a different *job* for content:

> [!example] The four quadrants
> | Quadrant | Axes | Goal | Psychology | Formats |
> |---|---|---|---|---|
> | **ENTERTAIN** | Emotional + Awareness | Grab attention (attention economy), generate buzz | Low commitment, high engagement — consumed for *fun*, not to buy yet | Viral videos (TikTok trends), quizzes & games, competitions, branded films |
> | **INSPIRE** | Emotional + Purchase | Build an emotional connection that leads to *preference* | Moves from *"I like this content"* to *"I want to be part of this brand"* — ties product to self-image/aspiration | Celebrity/influencer endorsements, community forums, reviews & ratings (social proof) |
> | **EDUCATE** | Rational + Awareness | Solve problems, build authority | User has a pain point, wants information not a pitch — builds trust (*"Youtility"*) | Infographics, how-to guides & tutorials, trend reports/e-books, press releases |
> | **CONVINCE** | Rational + Purchase | Justify the purchase with *facts* | User is interested but risk-averse; needs "proof" to justify **ROI** to self or boss | Case studies, webinars & product demos, datasheets/pricing calculators, interactive demos |

*Worked examples on the slides:* IKEA **ThisAbles** (accessibility add-ons for the BILLY shelf) as **Inspire**; a Casper *"10 Dog Sleeping Positions"* article as **Educate**. The final matrix slide plots ~25 formats across the four quadrants (viral/quizzes/branded videos top-left; celebrity endorsements/reviews top-right; infographics/guides/press releases bottom-left; case studies/webinars/calculations bottom-right).

## (b) Google's 3H model — Hero / Hub / Help (Hygiene)
Organises content by **cadence and usage** rather than by stage. A pyramid: a rare cinematic "Hero" tip, regular "Hub" content in the middle, always-on "Help" content as the base.

> [!example] Hero · Hub · Help
> | | **HERO** | **HUB** | **HELP / HYGIENE** |
> |---|---|---|---|
> | **Concept** | Large-scale "Go Big" push | Regular, engaging, community | Useful, evergreen, "always-on" |
> | **Purpose** | Mass awareness + emotional connection | Stay connected: retain audience, build community | Be found: utility answers to high-intent search |
> | **Frequency** | 1–2× per year | Weekly / episodic | Always-on |
> | **Primary goal** | Awareness / brand lift | Retention / community | SEO / utility / trust |
> | **Distribution** | Paid media / PR | Owned media / email | Search / social support |
> | **Production** | High / cinematic | Medium / templated | Low / functional |
> | **Emotional driver** | Awe / inspiration | Belonging / interest | Relief / gratitude |
> | **Audience** | Mass market | Current customers / fans | High-intent searchers |
>
> **The mindset shift:** the framework moves you from *"broadcasting"* (what we want to say) to *"serving"* (what the audience needs). Slide examples: an Orange France football film (Hero), IKEA "cook this page" (Hub/Help).

## (c) Content Properties
The third lens tags each piece by its **attributes** — a Venn of overlapping circles on the slide: **Date · Format · Brand · Target audiences · Time · Channel · Co-Op · Profession**. Planning by properties lets you slice a content library many ways (by persona, by season, by channel). Three governing principles close the section:

> [!quote] The three principles (Content Properties slide)
> 1. **Utility wins** — being genuinely useful builds more resilient brand assets and loyalty.
> 2. **Creativity solves problems** — the best creative solves real business and human challenges.
> 3. **Context is king** — the psychology of engagement is universal, but execution must be tailored to your audience, culture and goals.
>
> Illustrated by **Spotify Wrapped** (persona → need → relevance: your own year in data, hyper-personalized) and *Startup for Startup* / Google Skills as content-led brand-building.

# Part 5 — Planning, calendars & tools

## Why a content strategy at all
The deck lists the payoffs of having a strategy rather than ad-hoc posting: **business alignment, resource efficiency, customer-journey support, innovation, consistency (one brand voice), audience targeting, content quality.** A simple 5-step loop frames execution: **Audit → Goals → Plan → Create → Measure** (audit existing content and gaps, set business-aligned objectives, build the editorial calendar, produce assets, then track KPIs and optimise on data).

## Calendars & tools
> [!example] From the calendar slides
> - **Marketing Calendar (30-day):** aim for **at least 15 discrete activities** tied to Pre-Launch & Launch; work in **bursts — Test, Measure, Review.**
> - A **weekly editorial calendar** colour-codes content types (holiday campaign, e-book, webinar, blog post, SlideShare, product launch, experiment).
> - **Tools:** HubSpot's **Blog Ideas Generator** for topic ideation; the **December brainstorm** exercise shows how to mine a single month for angles (holidays, winter, end-of-fiscal-year, year/decade-end, entertainment).
> - **Remember McLuhan — "the medium is the message"** (a callback): the same content behaves differently per channel. **58%** of people admit to *"having a different experience from the same brand depending on the channel"* (Thunderhead) — hence the "each social platform is different" donut/Dolly-Parton/Friends memes (LinkedIn vs Facebook vs Instagram vs Tinder).

## The Periodic Table of Content Marketing (Econsultancy)
A single reference slide: Econsultancy's **Periodic Table of Content Marketing** organises every element of the discipline into colour-coded groups — **Strategy, Format, Content type, Platform, Metrics, Sharing triggers, Checklist, Goals** — with a **seven-step guide to success**: (1) define a strategy, (2) figure out formats, (3) research content types that appeal to your audience, (4) share across distribution/social platforms, (5) track metrics and map them to goals, (6) mind the **sharing triggers** ("work the emotions"), (7) always double-check your work. Treat it as a checklist poster for planning a content programme.

# Part 6 — Trends and AI

## Content trends
> [!example] Trends in content marketing (HubSpot State of Marketing 2024)
> Plotted on adoption × impact:
> - **Interactive content** — widely used but *less* impactful.
> - **Short-form video dominance** — highly adopted *and* impactful (the standout).
> - **Audio content growth** — widely adopted and impactful.
> - **Hyper-personalization & AI-driven content** — impactful but *not yet* widely adopted (the opportunity).

## AI in content marketing
The deck asks the obvious question — *"why not just use GenAI?"* — and answers with a **critical view of AI's limits** before its opportunities. (Foundational GenAI concepts are owned by the AI lectures; see → [[Digital Marketing Lecture 8]] and beyond.)

> [!warning] AI limitations — Generic · Explicit · Myopic (Kemp, 2024)
> From Kemp, A. (2024), *Competitive Advantage Through Artificial Intelligence: Toward a Theory of Situated AI* (Academy of Management Review):
> - **Generic** — the logic emerging from an AI algorithm *"can be rediscovered by anyone using the same procedure."* No durable edge.
> - **Explicit** — *"AI is a form of explicit knowledge… likely to be widely adopted among competing firms."* It diffuses fast, so it doesn't differentiate.
> - **Myopic** — AI *lacks contextual awareness of broader firm strategy*; it may optimise a sub-task while **harming the brand**.
>
> The strategic lesson: if everyone can generate the same content the same way, AI content is **commoditised** — competitive advantage comes from the human context AI can't see.

> [!tip] Opportunities — the human/machine split
> The closing "AI in (content) marketing" slide splits the work: **AI is strong on patterns, precision, speed, implementation, scale**; humans keep **creativity, innovation, imagination, judgement, morals & ethics.** AI **scales** content operations (ChatGPT ideation, HubSpot × Jasper *"Using GenAI to Scale Your Content Operations"*); humans supply the strategy, taste and ethics that keep it on-brand and non-generic.

# Part 7 — Influencer marketing (intro)

The deck ends by opening the influencer topic — a fuller treatment of **UGC virality, native advertising and STEPPS** is owned by → [[Digital Marketing Lecture 8]].

> [!example] Doggy Vacay Tour
> The **Doggy Vacay Tour** case shows influencer marketing in miniature: pet micro-influencers on Instagram (@ipartywithbrucewayne ~60k, @remixthedog ~125k, @iggyjoey ~55k) brought together for a branded, red-carpet "dog vacation" event in Israel. The takeaway: influence doesn't require celebrity reach — a **well-defined niche audience** and platform-native content (Instagram photo-led glamour) can carry a campaign. It also reinforces the **platform-fit** point — the content that works on Instagram (aspirational imagery) is not the content that works on LinkedIn or TikTok.

## Exam takeaways
> [!tip] What this lecture sets up for the past papers
> - **Content marketing = the penetrated-market / relevance tool.** Retention & Win-Back content nurtures *existing* customers; the stage dictates whether content is acquisition or retention. (Ties back to the "content marketing in the penetrated market" MCQ — see [[2024 Exam B1]].)
> - **Damian Ryan — "levelling the playing field":** content marketing *lowers the barriers of entry* and is cost-effective, offering **engagement instead of trying to sell** — appears as an MCQ. See [[2024 Exam B1]].
