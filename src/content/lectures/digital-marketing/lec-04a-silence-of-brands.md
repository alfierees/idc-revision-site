# Silence of the Brands

> Part of: [[Digital Marketing]]
> **Topic 4 — Brand Identity & Storytelling** · Reading
> Citation: Salzer-Mörling, M. & Strannegård, L. (2004). Silence of the brands. *European Journal of Marketing*, 38(1/2), 224–238. https://doi.org/10.1108/03090560410511203
> Key concepts: [[Brand Identity]], [[Brand Storytelling]], [[Brandscape]], [[Brand Aesthetics]], [[Co-Creation]]

---

## TL;DR
This conceptual paper challenges the dominant view of brands as **corporately managed stories** told by senders to passive receivers. The authors argue that brands no longer primarily *speak* through verbal narratives — they *show* through visual images. Branding is therefore better understood as a process of **aesthetic expression** built on feeling, impression and immersion rather than on meaning and persuasion. Because consumers actively use, re-use and even subvert brand signs (skinheads in Fred Perry, adbusters distorting logos), brand meaning is an *outcome* of joint construction in a "brandscape", not a starting point owned and controlled by the firm.

## Why It's on the Reading List
It is the critical, theory-led counterpoint for the brand identity / storytelling topic: it teaches you to question the neat "sender → message → receiver" model of branding and to think about brands as visual, aesthetic and co-constructed — a useful lens for exam questions on what a brand "really is" and who controls its meaning.

## Background & Research Question
Since the late 1980s brands have moved to centre stage: from a mere **marker** identifying a producer to becoming the product that is consumed. In an economy moving "from substance to image" (Alvesson, 1990), the production of *signs* matters more than the production of objects. The mainstream branding literature treats the brand as the firm's most crucial asset — an equity to be expressed through persuasive stories about core values.

The authors identify a gap: both the managerial branding literature *and* critical cultural studies cast the consumer as a passive receiver of pre-defined branded stories. Their aim is to re-conceptualise branding as aesthetic expression in which the conventional distinction between senders and receivers becomes blurred. The guiding questions: How are brand expressions created? Can they be controlled by the firm? What are the roles of producers and consumers in constructing expressive brands?

## Key Concepts & Definitions

> [!info] Definition — Brandscape
> A commercialised social landscape where the production and consumption of commercial icons takes place; "a battlefield of brand names, images and logos striving to be heard" (Christensen & Cheney, 2000). It is the arena in which brand images are expressed, impressed or suppressed.

> [!info] Definition — Mediascape
> Drawing on Appadurai (1996), the dimension of global cultural flows through which large repertoires of images (TV, internet, film, magazines) are disseminated. The mediascape is the *prerequisite* that makes the brandscape possible.

> [!info] Definition — Aesthetics (as used here)
> Not judgements of beauty or harmony (the classical sense) but sensory knowledge — from the Greek *aisth*, "to feel". An **aesthetic approach** to brands is grounded in feeling, intuition and immersion rather than logico-rational cognition.

Two contrasting modes of communication, drawing on Lash's (1988) reading of Lyotard:

| Mode | Nature | Concerned with | Brand parallel |
|------|--------|----------------|----------------|
| **Discursive (story)** | Verbal narrative | Meaning, cognition, interpretation | "Traditional" advertising telling value stories |
| **Figural (image)** | Visual / pictorial sign | Sensation, feeling, immersion | The brand as silent aesthetic icon |

The conventional **brand equity** model (Aaker, 1996) is summarised as four asset categories:

- brand name awareness
- brand loyalty
- perceived quality
- brand associations

## Main Arguments / Findings

**1. The mainstream literature is "single-voiced" and audial.** Brand theory (Aaker, Kapferer, Keller, Melin, Apéria, Jensen) frames the brand-owner as a story-teller and the market as an audience. Concepts such as awareness, associations, loyalty and narration all assume a sender, a message and a receiver. **Brand identity** (Kapferer 1992; Aaker's core vs. extended identity) is positioned at the centre, fine-tuned to better communicate the desired message.

**2. Brands are seen, not heard.** The authors' central move: as the brand becomes the product itself (a Prada garment, an Adidas item as symbolic sign), it is less a *story* about values than an *image* of aesthetic expression. Branding shifts from producing information to producing impressions.

> [!example] Voice vs. vision
> Advertising often tells stories ("feel lonely? buy these chips and feel joy" — Czarniawska's three-element narrative). Brands, by contrast, are highly abstract figural signs whose value lies in the impressions they make, not the meanings they create. The rise of *image* over *story* is what distinguishes traditional marketing from the emerging brandscape.

**3. A third stance: double agency.** Against both brand-cheerleaders (Olins: brands are "the greatest gift commerce has brought to popular culture") and the doom-laden critics (Klein's *No Logo*; a "giant global Disney World inhabited by silenced citizens"), the authors propose focusing on the **double agency** of the brandscape. Consumers are not a passive audience: signs become value-laden brands only when consumers absorb, immerse in and *use* them. Consumption is a form of **aesthetic reflexivity** (Lash & Urry) through which people construct identities — "I shop therefore I am".

> [!example] Brands escape corporate control
> - **Fred Perry**: marketed to sporty upper-middle-class consumers, yet for decades worn by skinheads as a sign of belonging — a meaning the firm never intended.
> - **Cultural jamming / adbusters**: activists "subvertise" by hijacking billboards and distorting logos (e.g. adding "true" to "The Colors of Benetton"), using the brands' own aesthetic language to critique corporate hypocrisy.

> [!warning] Implication
> Any story or meaning a brand carries is an *outcome* of contextual, social use — never a fixed, ready-made narrative waiting to be unfolded. Corporations can never fully control how brand expressions are created or recontextualised.

## Framework / Model
The paper is conceptual, not empirical, but it advances a clear re-framing rather than a testable model: branding moves from a **narrative/sender model** (firm crafts a story → audience receives meaning) to an **aesthetic/expressive model** (firm distributes silent visual signs → signs are consumed, contextualised and mobilised by reflexive consumers → meaning emerges as an effect). Drawing on Baudrillard, consumption is a "systematic manipulation of signs": on the sign-value level objects can be substituted infinitely, and consumers use brands however they like.

## Implications for Marketers

- Branding is increasingly about producing **visual impressions** and aesthetic experiences, not just communicating verbal value propositions.
- The firm is **not in full control** of its brand's meaning; brands live in a social context and are re-appropriated by consumers and activists.
- Investing in tightly controlled "corporate stories" can backfire when those moralised values are not lived up to (vulnerability to scrutiny, cultural jamming).
- Think of consumers as **active co-producers** of brand meaning, capable of subverting intended signals.

## Exam Takeaways
> [!tip] Likely exam points
> - The core thesis: brands are **silent** — they communicate via *figural* images (sensation/immersion), not *discursive* stories (meaning/persuasion).
> - Distinguish the **sender→message→receiver** model (mainstream brand theory) from the **double-agency / co-construction** view.
> - Define **brandscape**, **mediascape**, and **aesthetic reflexivity**.
> - Key examples: **Fred Perry / skinheads** (uncontrolled appropriation) and **adbusters / cultural jamming** (subversion).
> - Aaker's four brand-equity assets (awareness, loyalty, perceived quality, associations) — used here as the model being critiqued.
> - "Meaning is an effect rather than a starting point."

## Summary
- Brands have shifted from markers of origin to products consumed in their own right.
- Mainstream branding literature treats brands as managed stories; the authors reject this as too "single-voiced".
- Branding is reframed as aesthetic expression: images over stories, impression over meaning.
- Consumers actively use, re-use and subvert brand signs — meaning is jointly constructed in the brandscape.
- Corporate control over brand meaning is therefore volatile and incomplete.
