# Embedding the Marketing Angle into the Pursuit of Entrepreneurial Propensity: Roles of Perceived Feasibility and Desirability and Stakeholders' Support

> Part of: [[Digital Marketing]]
> **Topic 1 — Marketing 101 / The Shift to Digital** · Reading
> Citation: Kariv, D., Krueger, N., Cisneros, L., & Kashy-Rosenbaum, G. (2025). Embedding the marketing angle into the pursuit of entrepreneurial propensity: roles of perceived feasibility and desirability and stakeholders' support. *International Journal of Entrepreneurial Behavior & Research, 31*(2/3), 587–608.
> Key concepts: [[Marketing Capabilities]], [[Entrepreneurial Propensity]], [[Perceived Feasibility]], [[Perceived Desirability]], [[Stakeholder Support]], [[Theory of Planned Behaviour]]

---

## TL;DR
This study asks *why* some people with entrepreneurial intentions actually start a business while others never act. The authors argue the missing ingredient is the **"content"** of the classic psychological antecedents — feasibility and desirability — and that this content is largely supplied by an individual's perceived **marketing capabilities**. Using a sample of 1,957 Quebec respondents split into three propensity groups, they show that marketing-fuelled feasibility (**Market Feasibility**) and desirability (**Market Desirability**) strongly differentiate doers from non-doers, and that the *type* and *function* of stakeholder support (institution vs person; bridging vs buffering) further shape both intentions and action. The marketing angle, in short, is what turns intention into action.

## Why It's on the Reading List
It anchors **Topic 1 (Marketing 101 / the shift to digital)** by grounding the course in what marketing capability actually *is* — market sensing, communications, planning and product development — and why it is foundational, not cosmetic. It teaches the exam-relevant idea that marketing is a strategic capability that builds confidence and drives action, framed through well-known behavioural theories.

## Background & Research Question
New ventures face fierce competition and must constantly build marketing capabilities to survive. Yet research has rarely treated marketing capabilities as a *catalyst* of entrepreneurial action. The dominant intention–action theories — Ajzen's [[Theory of Planned Behaviour]] (TPB) and Shapero & Sokol's [[Entrepreneurial Event Model]] (EEM) — predict that intentions convert to action when people perceive the act as **feasible** and **desirable**. But these constructs are defined so abstractly that they often fail to predict who actually acts.

The paper addresses three gaps:

1. Feasibility/desirability alone do not capture the full scope of [[Entrepreneurial Propensity]]; the stakeholder perspective is usually studied separately.
2. The "content" that gives feasibility and desirability their motivating power is under-specified — the authors propose **marketing capabilities** as that content.
3. [[Stakeholder Support]] is rarely analysed by *type* (institution / person) and *function* (bridging / buffering), nor linked to feasibility and desirability.

## Key Concepts & Definitions

> [!info] Definition — Marketing capabilities
> Marketing orientation, skills, know-how and mechanisms — including market information management, marketing communications, market planning and new product development — that let founders read market signals and respond quickly. Drawn from Vorhies & Morgan (2005).

> [!info] Definition — Entrepreneurial propensity
> The transformation of entrepreneurial *intentions* into *action* (starting a business). Treated here not as a yes/no act but as a spectrum across three groups.

> [!info] Definition — Perceived feasibility vs desirability
> **Feasibility** = self-belief in having the abilities/skills to pursue the venture (akin to perceived behavioural control / self-efficacy). **Desirability** = belief in the overall attractiveness of the entrepreneurship outcome.

The authors "fuel" the abstract constructs with marketing capability to create two composite variables:

| Composite | Built from | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **Market Feasibility (MF)** | Feasibility × marketing capabilities | "I have the marketing know-how to make this work" |
| **Market Desirability (MD)** | Desirability × marketing capabilities | "Marketing-grounded entrepreneurship is attractive to me" |

### Stakeholder support: a 2×2 classification

| | **Bridging** (open doors, connect, share knowledge) | **Buffering** (protect, regulate, structure) |
|---|---|---|
| **People** (mentors, peers, family) | Bridging-P | Buffering-P |
| **Institutions** (banks, incubators, government) | Bridging-I | Buffering-I |

> [!info] Definition — Bridging vs buffering
> **Bridging** support facilitates collaborations and resource access (collaborative, experimental). **Buffering** support protects the venture via rules, regulations and bureaucracy (structured, often institutional).

## Main Arguments / Findings

The three propensity groups (the dependent variable):

| Code | Group | n | % |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | Employees, **no** intentions, no action | 728 | 37.2% |
| 1 | Employees, **with** intentions, no action | 664 | 33.9% |
| 2 | **Novice entrepreneurs** (intentions + action) | 565 | 28.9% |

Key results:

- **MF, MD and Bridging-P were the strongest discriminators** between groups; in discriminant analysis MF had the highest loading (0.959), then MD (0.576), then Bridging-P (0.465).
- Novice entrepreneurs held significantly higher MF, MD and Bridging-P, and *lower* Buffering-I, than non-entrepreneurs.
- **Psychological components (MF, MD) separate action from non-action**, but they did **not** separate the two non-action groups from each other. What separated *intenders* from *non-intenders* was the **market-support** constructs (higher Bridging-P, lower Buffering-I).
- MF had a positive **indirect** effect on propensity *through* MD (mediation): marketing-grounded capability raises desirability, which raises action.

> [!example] The bridging/buffering twist
> Buffering-I (structured institutional support) **positively moderated** the MD → propensity link: people high in desirability were even more likely to act when also receiving structured institutional support. So bureaucratic support isn't purely a deterrent — once someone wants to act, structure can reinforce the leap.

> [!warning] Intentions ≠ action
> The robust finding is that lumping all non-entrepreneurs together hides the mechanism. Intentions and action are driven by *different* factors — psychological content for action, stakeholder support for intentions — so they must be modelled separately.

## Methodology

- **Data:** Quebec Entrepreneurial Index (2019), online questionnaire; n = 1,957 adults (47% male), quota-sampled and weighted.
- **Design:** Three pre-assigned groups (above). Dependent variable = ordinal propensity (0–2).
- **Measures:** Validated Likert scales — feasibility (8 items, α = 0.92), desirability (6 items, α = 0.95), marketing capabilities (4 items, α = 0.95), and 6 stakeholder-support items factor-analysed into the 2×2 (bridging/buffering × people/institution). MF and MD computed by combining feasibility/desirability with marketing capabilities.
- **Analysis:** Discriminant analysis (group differences) + Hayes PROCESS Model 28 (serial mediation + moderation), bootstrapped (5,000 samples), controlling for gender and age. Direct/mediated/moderated model: R² = 0.39.

## Framework / Model

The proposed integrative model fuses three literatures:

1. **TPB + EEM** (psychological antecedents) →
2. **Marketing capabilities** as the "content" fuelling feasibility/desirability →
3. **Stakeholder theory**, refined into a type × function classification.

Logical chain (PROCESS): **MF → MD → Entrepreneurial Propensity**, with **Bridging-P** strengthening the MF → MD path and **Buffering-I** strengthening the MD → propensity path.

## Implications for Marketers

- **Marketing capability is a confidence engine.** Building genuine market-sensing, communications, planning and product-development skills raises self-efficacy and makes founders more likely to act.
- **Higher education and ecosystems should transmit marketing knowledge** to novice entrepreneurs via applied learning, internships, mentors and role models.
- **Match support to stage:** people-based bridging support is most valuable early (it builds desirability); structured institutional support can reinforce action once desire is high.
- **"Knowledge of market signals is power."** Marketing know-how is positioned as the practical content that converts aspiration into a launched venture.

## Exam Takeaways
> [!tip] Likely exam points
> - Marketing capabilities = market information management, marketing communications, market planning, product development (Vorhies & Morgan).
> - Feasibility = "can I?" (self-efficacy); Desirability = "do I want to?" (attractiveness). TPB (Ajzen) + EEM (Shapero & Sokol) underpin both.
> - The 2×2 stakeholder grid: **bridging vs buffering** × **people vs institutions**.
> - Core finding: psychological/marketing content separates **action from non-action**; stakeholder support separates **intenders from non-intenders**.
> - Mediation: MF → MD → propensity. Moderation: Bridging-P on MF→MD; Buffering-I on MD→propensity.

## Summary
- Reframes feasibility and desirability by giving them marketing "content."
- Marketing-grounded feasibility (MF) and desirability (MD) strongly predict who acts.
- Stakeholder support is best understood as a type × function matrix.
- Intentions and action have different drivers and must be studied as a spectrum, not a binary.
